# Emergency Procedures for Crisis Zones

## Active Shelling and Airstrikes

**Immediate Actions:**
- Seek the lowest floor of the nearest solid building immediately
- Stay away from windows and exterior walls - glass shards and debris are major hazards
- If in a basement or shelter, position yourself under sturdy furniture or against interior walls
- Cover your head and neck with your arms or any protective material available
- Remain in shelter until the all-clear signal or at least 30 minutes after the last explosion

**Critical Timing:**
- You have approximately 15-30 seconds from hearing an air raid siren to reach cover
- If caught in the open during shelling, drop to the ground immediately and seek any depression in terrain
- Do not attempt to run long distances - the nearest minimal cover is safer than being exposed

**Building Selection Priority:**
1. Reinforced concrete structures with basements
2. Buildings away from military targets, fuel depots, or government facilities
3. Structures with multiple floors (middle floors if basement unavailable)
4. Avoid high-rise buildings above 5 floors - structural damage risk increases

## Gunfire and Active Shooting

**Drop and Cover Protocol:**
- Immediately drop flat to the ground at the first sound of gunfire
- Do not stand or crouch - your profile must be as low as possible
- Crawl to the nearest hard cover (concrete walls, vehicle engine blocks, thick trees)
- Soft cover (wooden doors, car doors, bushes) conceals you but does not stop bullets

**Understanding Ballistics:**
- Bullets can penetrate multiple walls depending on caliber
- High-velocity rounds travel faster than sound - you may be hit before hearing shots
- Ricochet danger is highest in urban environments with hard surfaces
- Stay low - most gunfire in urban combat occurs at standing height

**Movement Under Fire:**
- Move only when absolutely necessary and during lulls in shooting
- Keep low and use crawling techniques
- Move from cover to cover in short bursts (3-5 seconds maximum)
- Never silhouette yourself against lit backgrounds or open sky

## Evacuation Procedures

**Essential Preparation (Pack in 5 Minutes):**
- Identity documents (passport, ID cards, birth certificates) in waterproof bag
- Critical medications with prescriptions
- Phone, charger, power bank
- Water (1 liter per person minimum)
- Cash in small denominations and local currency
- One change of practical clothing and sturdy footwear

**Route Planning:**
- Know at least two evacuation routes from your location
- Monitor official evacuation corridor announcements via radio or trusted authorities
- Travel during announced humanitarian corridors or ceasefires when possible
- Mark your route on a physical map - digital navigation may fail

**Group Movement Safety:**
- Never evacuate alone if possible - groups of 3-7 are optimal
- Assign roles: navigator, rear guard, children/elderly supervision
- Maintain visual contact with all group members
- Establish a rally point if separated

**Indicators to Evacuate Immediately:**
- Direct order from legitimate authorities or humanitarian organizations
- Imminent ground assault in your area (announced or observed)
- Building structural damage compromising safety
- Lack of water/food for more than 3 days with no resupply route
- Serious medical emergency requiring evacuation for treatment

## Communication During Emergencies

**Primary Communication Channels:**
- Local emergency numbers (commit to memory): 
  - Medical: varies by country (103 in Ukraine, 101 in Egypt, check local)
  - Fire: varies by country
  - Police: varies by country
- International humanitarian hotlines (save in phone before crisis)
- Community radio frequencies (often broadcast in local language during crisis)

**Signal Protocols:**
- White cloth or flag indicates surrender/non-combatant status in most contexts
- Red cross/crescent indicates medical facility (do not misuse - war crime)
- Flashing lights in windows can signal distress to observers
- Three of anything (whistles, light flashes, gunshots) is international distress signal

**Digital Communication Security:**
- Assume all digital communication is monitored
- Use end-to-end encrypted apps (Signal, WhatsApp) for sensitive information
- Share locations only with trusted individuals through secure channels
- Phone batteries are precious - use sparingly and recharge when possible

**Satellite Phone Guidelines:**
- If you have access to satellite phone, use for critical emergency communication only
- Satellite phones can be expensive - keep calls brief
- Line of sight to sky required - use outdoors or near windows
- Save critical numbers in phone memory before emergency

## Shelter-in-Place Protocols

**When to Shelter Instead of Evacuate:**
- Active combat in your immediate area making movement deadly
- No safe evacuation route available
- Extreme weather conditions (sandstorm, winter blizzard)
- Official orders to shelter in place
- Chemical/biological weapon threat (seal room immediately)

**Room Sealing for Chemical Threats:**
- Select an interior room on highest floor possible (gases heavier than air sink)
- Seal all windows and doors with plastic sheeting and tape
- Turn off all ventilation, heating, and air conditioning systems
- Place wet towels at door gaps
- Monitor official channels for all-clear before unsealing

**Minimum Supplies for 72-Hour Shelter:**
- Water: 3 liters per person per day (9 liters per person total)
- Non-perishable food requiring no cooking
- First aid kit with trauma supplies
- Battery-powered radio
- Flashlight with extra batteries
- Sanitation supplies (plastic bags, toilet paper, hygiene items)
- Important documents in waterproof container

**Psychological Considerations:**
- Maintain routine activities to reduce stress
- Keep children occupied with quiet activities
- Limit exposure to distressing news
- Check on neighbors and vulnerable individuals if safe to do so
- Conserve energy and resources - crisis duration is often unpredictable

## Post-Incident Safety

**Unexploded Ordnance (UXO) Awareness:**
- Never touch or approach any unknown object after shelling/bombing
- UXO can include bombs, shells, grenades, landmines, cluster munitions
- Mark location from safe distance and report to authorities
- Teach children: "See something strange - don't touch, walk away, tell adult"
- Characteristic signs: metallic objects, unusual shapes in rubble, trip wires

**Structural Safety Assessment:**
- Do not enter buildings with visible structural damage (cracks, leaning, collapsed sections)
- Check for gas leaks (smell of gas, hissing sounds) before entering
- Beware of damaged electrical wires - assume all wires are live
- Fire hazard increases after bombardment - have evacuation plan even in shelter

**Documenting Incidents:**
- If safe, photograph/video damage for documentation and potential legal accountability
- Note date, time, and location of incidents
- Preserve any fragments or evidence safely (without touching UXO)
- Report to humanitarian organizations or documentation groups
- Your documentation may help bring accountability for war crimes

## Medical Emergency Response

**Immediate Life-Threatening Priorities (ABC):**
- **A**irway: Ensure person can breathe (clear obstruction, head tilt-chin lift)
- **B**reathing: Check for breathing, begin rescue breathing if needed
- **C**irculation: Control severe bleeding with direct pressure

**Triage in Mass Casualty Events:**
- Prioritize those who will die without immediate intervention but can be saved
- Immediate: Severe bleeding, airway obstruction, tension pneumothorax
- Delayed: Fractures, moderate burns, non-life-threatening injuries
- Minimal: Walking wounded who can wait
- Expectant: Unsurvivable injuries (in resource-scarce environments)

**Transport to Medical Facility:**
- Improvised stretcher from blanket/jacket and two poles
- Spinal immobilization critical if fall/blast injury suspected
- Control bleeding during transport
- Keep patient warm to prevent shock
- Note time of injury and treatments applied

Remember: Your life is valuable. Do not take unnecessary risks. Follow instructions from legitimate humanitarian organizations and local authorities during crisis situations.
