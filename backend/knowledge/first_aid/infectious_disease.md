# First Aid: Infectious Disease Outbreaks

## Overview

Infectious disease outbreaks are one of the deadliest secondary effects of conflict and displacement. When water systems fail, sanitation collapses, vaccination programs are disrupted, and people are crowded into shelters, diseases spread rapidly. In crisis settings, more people often die from infectious disease outbreaks than from the conflict itself. Early recognition, basic isolation, and supportive care save lives.

## Measles

### Why It Matters in Crisis Zones
Measles is one of the most contagious diseases known - one infected person can spread it to 12-18 others. In malnourished, unvaccinated populations in crisis settings, measles kills. It is a leading cause of death in refugee camps and displaced populations.

### Recognition
**Early signs (Day 1-3 of illness):**
- High fever (often 40°C / 104°F or higher)
- Runny nose, cough, red watery eyes
- General feeling of illness, fatigue
- Small white spots inside the cheeks (Koplik spots) - a distinctive early sign

**Rash (Day 3-7):**
- Red, blotchy rash starting on the face and behind the ears
- Spreads downward to neck, trunk, arms, and legs over 3 days
- Rash lasts 5-6 days, then fades in the same order it appeared
- Fever often spikes when rash appears

**Contagious Period:** From 4 days BEFORE rash appears to 4 days AFTER rash appears. This means people spread the disease before they know they have it.

### Immediate Steps
1. **Isolate the person** - keep away from others, especially unvaccinated children, pregnant women, and immunocompromised people
2. **Vitamin A supplementation** - WHO recommends two doses given 24 hours apart. This reduces mortality and prevents blindness:
   - Infants under 6 months: 50,000 IU per dose
   - Infants 6-11 months: 100,000 IU per dose
   - Children 12 months and older: 200,000 IU per dose
3. **Control fever** - give paracetamol (acetaminophen) for comfort. Do NOT give aspirin to children.
4. **Maintain hydration** - plenty of fluids, ORS if diarrhea develops
5. **Continue feeding** - maintain nutritious diet, continue breastfeeding

### Complications to Watch For
- **Pneumonia** (most common cause of measles death) - worsening cough, rapid breathing, chest indrawing
- **Severe diarrhea/dehydration** - sunken eyes, no tears, reduced urine
- **Eye damage/blindness** - eye pain, discharge, vision changes
- **Encephalitis (brain inflammation)** - seizures, confusion, severe headache, stiff neck
- **Ear infection** - ear pain, discharge from ear

### Isolation Protocol
- Separate from others for at least 4 days after rash appears
- In crowded shelters: create a separate area, even a curtained-off space
- Caregivers should be people who have been vaccinated or had measles before
- Ventilate the room (open windows) - measles virus is airborne

## Cholera

See the dedicated **Cholera and Waterborne Diseases** guide for comprehensive cholera protocols.

**Quick Reference:**
- Profuse watery diarrhea (rice-water stool), vomiting
- Can kill within hours through dehydration
- Treatment: aggressive oral rehydration (ORS), IV fluids for severe cases
- Prevention: clean water, handwashing, sanitation
- Highly contagious through contaminated water and food

## Typhoid Fever

### Recognition
- **Prolonged high fever** (rising over days, can reach 40°C / 104°F) - this is the hallmark
- Headache, fatigue, body aches
- Abdominal pain, often with constipation (less commonly diarrhea)
- Rose-colored spots on the chest or abdomen (not always present)
- Confusion or delirium in severe cases
- Symptoms develop gradually over 1-3 weeks after exposure

### Immediate Steps
1. **Seek medical care** - typhoid requires antibiotic treatment
2. **Control fever** with paracetamol (acetaminophen)
3. **Maintain hydration** - plenty of clean fluids
4. **Continue feeding** - small, frequent, easily digestible meals
5. **Isolate the person's waste** - typhoid spreads through feces. Strict handwashing after using latrine.

### Critical Warning
- Even after recovery, some people become **chronic carriers** - they shed bacteria in their stool for weeks to months
- Rigorous handwashing and hygiene must continue even after the person feels better
- Medical confirmation that bacteria are eliminated is ideal before the person prepares food for others

## Hepatitis A and E

### Why They Matter
Common in crisis settings where sanitation is disrupted. Hepatitis E is particularly dangerous for pregnant women (up to 25% mortality in third trimester).

### Recognition
- Fatigue, nausea, loss of appetite, abdominal pain (right upper area)
- Dark urine (tea-colored)
- Pale/clay-colored stool
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes) - develops after several days
- Fever (usually low-grade)

### Immediate Steps
1. **Rest** - the liver needs time to recover
2. **Hydration** - plenty of fluids
3. **Avoid alcohol** completely
4. **Strict hygiene** - disease spreads through fecal-oral route
5. **Monitor pregnant women** with hepatitis E very closely - seek medical care urgently

## Acute Respiratory Infections (Pneumonia)

### Why It Matters
The leading infectious cause of death in children under 5 in crisis settings, especially when combined with malnutrition and crowded living conditions.

### Recognition
- Rapid breathing (most reliable sign):
  - Under 2 months: 60+ breaths/minute
  - 2-12 months: 50+ breaths/minute
  - 1-5 years: 40+ breaths/minute
  - Adults: 30+ breaths/minute
- Chest indrawing (ribs pull inward with each breath)
- Fever, cough
- Unable to drink or breastfeed (danger sign in children)
- Blue lips or fingertips (severe - immediate danger)

### Immediate Steps
1. **Count breathing rate** carefully for a full minute
2. **Seek medical care** - pneumonia usually requires antibiotics
3. **Keep hydrated** - fluids help thin mucus
4. **Keep warm but not overheated**
5. **Position** sitting up or slightly propped up to ease breathing
6. **Continue breastfeeding** for infants - breast milk provides antibodies

## General Infection Prevention in Crisis Settings

### Handwashing (Most Important Single Intervention)
- Wash with soap and water for at least 20 seconds
- Critical times: after using latrine, before eating, before preparing food, after caring for sick people, after changing diapers
- If no soap: use ash mixed with water (traditional and effective alternative)
- Alcohol-based hand sanitizer (60%+ alcohol) if available

### Water and Food Safety
- Boil drinking water for 1 minute (3 minutes at altitude above 2000m)
- If cannot boil: chlorinate (2 drops household bleach per liter, wait 30 minutes) or use purification tablets
- Store clean water in covered containers with narrow openings
- Cook food thoroughly, eat while hot
- Wash fruits and vegetables with clean water, peel when possible

### Sanitation
- Use designated latrines - never defecate near water sources or living areas
- Latrines should be at least 30 meters from water sources
- Bury human waste if no latrine available (at least 30 cm deep)
- Keep living areas clean, dispose of waste regularly

### Isolation Principles (When Someone Gets Sick)
1. **Separate the sick person** from others as much as possible, especially vulnerable people
2. **Designate one caregiver** if possible - reduces exposure to others
3. **Caregiver hygiene:** Wash hands before and after every contact
4. **Separate utensils:** The sick person should have their own cup, plate, and utensils
5. **Clean surfaces** that the sick person touches regularly
6. **Ventilate** the sick area - open windows, allow air circulation
7. **Dispose of waste** (vomit, diarrhea, used tissues) safely - bury or burn

### Vaccination When Available
Prioritize vaccination campaigns in crisis settings for:
- Measles (most urgent - highly contagious and deadly in malnourished children)
- Cholera (oral vaccine, used in outbreaks and displacement camps)
- Typhoid (conjugate vaccine, single dose)
- Meningitis (in epidemic-prone regions)

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT use antibiotics without medical guidance** - wrong antibiotics do not help and contribute to resistance
- **Do NOT share personal items** (cups, towels, utensils) during outbreaks
- **Do NOT hide illness** - early reporting helps contain outbreaks
- **Do NOT stop treatment early** if antibiotics are prescribed - complete the full course
- **Do NOT give aspirin to children** with fever - use paracetamol/acetaminophen only
- **Do NOT assume diarrhea is "just diarrhea"** in outbreak settings - it can be cholera or dysentery
- **Do NOT ignore skin rashes** - they may indicate measles or other contagious diseases
- **Do NOT overcrowd sleeping areas** more than absolutely necessary - respiratory infections spread in crowds

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

**Immediately:**
- Any child under 5 with rapid breathing, chest indrawing, or inability to drink
- Profuse watery or bloody diarrhea
- High fever (above 39°C / 102°F) lasting more than 3 days
- Confusion, seizures, or loss of consciousness
- Severe dehydration (sunken eyes, skin pinch stays tented, no urine)
- Stiff neck with fever (possible meningitis)
- Jaundice (yellow skin/eyes) in a pregnant woman

**Within 24 Hours:**
- Any fever lasting more than 2 days
- Diarrhea lasting more than 1 day in children, 3 days in adults
- Rash with fever (possible measles)
- Painful urination with fever
- Ear discharge or severe ear pain

**Report to Health Authorities:**
- Multiple people in the same area with similar symptoms (possible outbreak)
- Any suspected cholera case (profuse watery diarrhea)
- Any suspected measles case (fever + rash)
- Cluster of deaths from unknown cause

Sources: WHO Measles Fact Sheet, WHO Typhoid Fact Sheet, WHO WASH guidelines for crisis settings, WHO Community Management of Infectious Disease protocols.
