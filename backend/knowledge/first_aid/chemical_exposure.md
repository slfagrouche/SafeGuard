# First Aid: Chemical Exposure

## Overview

Chemical attacks and industrial chemical releases are a reality in conflict zones. Chemical agents are designed to cause maximum harm and spread panic. The key to survival is rapid recognition and immediate decontamination. You do NOT need to identify the exact chemical to begin life-saving actions - the initial response is similar for most chemical exposures: get away, remove contaminated clothing, and flush with water.

**Critical Rule:** Protect yourself first. A rescuer who becomes contaminated becomes another victim. Approach from upwind (wind blowing from behind you toward the contamination) and stay uphill from liquid spills.

## Chemical Agent Categories and Recognition

### Nerve Agents (Sarin, VX, Tabun, Novichok)
**How they work:** Block nerve signals, causing muscles to contract uncontrollably and glands to overproduce fluids. Extremely dangerous - small amounts can be lethal.

**Recognition - SLUDGE Signs:**
- **S**alivation (excessive drooling)
- **L**acrimation (uncontrollable tearing)
- **U**rination (loss of bladder control)
- **D**efecation (loss of bowel control)
- **G**astrointestinal distress (nausea, vomiting, cramps)
- **E**mesis (vomiting)

**Additional signs:** Pinpoint pupils (miosis), eye pain, blurred vision, excessive sweating, muscle twitching, seizures, difficulty breathing, wheezing, confusion, loss of consciousness.

**Timeline:** Vapor exposure causes symptoms within seconds to minutes. Skin exposure may take minutes to hours.

### Blister Agents (Mustard Gas, Lewisite)
**How they work:** Damage skin, eyes, and airways on contact. Effects may be delayed hours after exposure.

**Recognition:**
- Mustard gas has a garlic or mustard-like smell
- Eye irritation, redness, and pain (often first symptom)
- Skin redness, itching, then blisters forming 2-24 hours after exposure
- Respiratory irritation: runny nose, cough, hoarseness, chest tightness
- Large fluid-filled blisters on skin, especially in warm/moist areas (armpits, groin)

**WARNING:** With mustard gas, there may be NO pain at the time of exposure. Symptoms appear hours later when damage is already done.

### Choking Agents (Chlorine, Phosgene)
**How they work:** Damage the lungs, causing them to fill with fluid (pulmonary edema).

**Recognition:**
- Chlorine: strong bleach/swimming pool smell, yellow-green gas
- Phosgene: smell of freshly cut hay or grass
- Immediate: coughing, choking, chest tightness, eye and throat irritation
- Delayed (2-24 hours): severe difficulty breathing, frothy sputum, blue skin
- Phosgene is particularly dangerous because initial symptoms may be mild, then severe lung damage develops hours later

### Blood Agents (Hydrogen Cyanide)
**How they work:** Prevent cells from using oxygen, causing rapid suffocation at the cellular level.

**Recognition:**
- Bitter almond smell (not everyone can detect this)
- Rapid breathing, then gasping
- Dizziness, headache, confusion
- Cherry-red skin color (sometimes)
- Seizures and rapid loss of consciousness
- Extremely fast-acting - minutes to death without treatment

## Immediate Steps (First 5 Minutes)

### 1. Protect Yourself
- **Do NOT enter a contaminated area** without proper protection
- Approach from **upwind and uphill**
- Cover your mouth and nose with a wet cloth (offers limited protection for choking agents)
- If you smell something unusual or see people collapsing, MOVE AWAY immediately

### 2. Remove the Victim from Exposure
- Move to fresh air immediately - get away from the source
- Move UPWIND (wind at your back) and UPHILL (gases tend to settle in low areas)
- If indoors, get outside. If outside, move away from the area.
- Speed matters more than direction - any movement away is better than staying.

### 3. Decontaminate (Remove the Chemical)
**This is the single most important treatment for most chemical exposures.**

**Remove Clothing:**
- Remove ALL clothing immediately - cut off rather than pulling over the head to avoid spreading contamination to face
- Clothing can trap and continue releasing chemical agents
- Removing clothing removes up to 80% of contamination
- Place removed clothing in a sealed plastic bag if possible, away from others
- Remove watches, jewelry, glasses - anything that may have trapped agent

**Flush with Water:**
- Flush entire body with large amounts of water for at least 15-20 minutes
- Eyes: flush continuously with clean water, holding eyelids open, for at least 15-20 minutes
- Use any available water - shower, hose, bottled water, even non-potable water is better than no flushing
- Flush from head downward so contaminated water runs away from face
- For solid/powder agents: brush off before flushing with water

**If No Water Available:**
- Use flour, talcum powder, or Fuller's earth to blot (not rub) liquid agents from skin
- Even dry wiping with absorbent cloth removes some agent

### 4. Basic Life Support
- If not breathing: open airway (head-tilt, chin-lift)
- If breathing but unconscious: recovery position (on side)
- If nerve agent suspected and person is seizing: protect from injury, clear area around them
- **Do NOT perform mouth-to-mouth resuscitation** if chemical contamination is suspected - use a barrier device or bag-valve mask only

## Ongoing Care Until Help Arrives

1. **Keep monitoring breathing.** Chemical lung damage can worsen over hours, especially with phosgene and chlorine.
2. **Keep the person calm and still.** Physical exertion worsens chemical lung injury.
3. **Position:** Sitting upright helps breathing. If unconscious, recovery position.
4. **Keep warm.** Chemical exposure can cause shock and hypothermia.
5. **For eye exposure:** Continue flushing eyes. Do not apply patches or bandages over eyes.
6. **For skin blisters (blister agents):** Do not pop blisters. Cover loosely with clean, dry dressing. Treat blisters like burns.
7. **Watch for delayed symptoms:** Phosgene and mustard gas can cause serious symptoms HOURS after exposure when the person initially felt better. Monitor for at least 24 hours.
8. **Decontaminate yourself** after helping - wash thoroughly, change your own clothing.

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT enter a contaminated area** to rescue someone without protection - you will become a casualty
- **Do NOT perform mouth-to-mouth** on a chemically contaminated person
- **Do NOT induce vomiting** if chemical was swallowed (unless specifically instructed by poison control)
- **Do NOT apply creams, ointments, or bandages** over chemically contaminated skin before flushing
- **Do NOT rub the skin** during decontamination - this can push chemicals deeper into tissue. Blot or flush.
- **Do NOT use hot water** for decontamination - warm or cool water only. Hot water opens pores and increases absorption.
- **Do NOT assume someone is safe** because initial symptoms are mild - especially with phosgene and mustard gas, the worst comes hours later
- **Do NOT let the person exert themselves** - rest reduces the rate of chemical absorption and lung damage
- **Do NOT delay decontamination** waiting for "proper" equipment - immediate water flushing with any available water is better than waiting

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

**Immediately (all chemical exposures):**
- Any suspected nerve agent exposure (muscle twitching, pinpoint pupils, excessive secretions, seizures)
- Difficulty breathing or chest tightness
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures
- Eye exposure of any kind

**Within Hours (even if feeling okay):**
- Any suspected phosgene or chlorine exposure - lung damage develops over 2-24 hours
- Any skin exposure to blister agents - damage continues to develop
- Persistent cough, sore throat, or eye irritation after exposure
- Anyone who was in an area where chemical agent was released, even briefly

**Ongoing Monitoring:**
- Anyone with chemical exposure should be monitored for at least 24 hours
- Worsening breathing, new blisters, or deteriorating consciousness needs immediate care

## Special Notes for Conflict Zone Chemical Attacks

- **Chlorine barrel bombs** in Syria-type conflicts: chlorine is heavier than air and settles in basements and low areas. Move upstairs and to higher ground.
- **White phosphorus:** Burns on contact with air. Submerge affected area in water or cover with wet cloth to exclude air. Pick out visible pieces with tweezers (they will reignite when dry). Keep wounds wet.
- **Tear gas (CS gas):** Not usually lethal but can be dangerous in enclosed spaces. Move to fresh air. Face into the wind with eyes open to help gas evaporate. Flush eyes with water. Do not rub eyes.
- **After any chemical attack:** Do not touch surfaces, objects, or dead animals in the affected area. Chemical residue can persist on surfaces for extended periods.

## Improvised Protection (Limited Effectiveness)

When no proper protective equipment is available:
- Wet cloth over nose and mouth (provides minimal filtering for some agents)
- Plastic sheeting or raincoat over body (temporary skin protection)
- Goggles or swimming goggles (eye protection)
- Rubber or plastic gloves
- These are NOT substitutes for proper protection but are better than nothing for brief exposure

Sources: CHEMM (Chemical Hazards Emergency Medical Management), WHO Chemical Incidents Fact Sheet, CDC Chemical Emergency guidelines, OPCW first responder guidance.
