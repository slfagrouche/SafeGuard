# First Aid: Malnutrition Emergency Treatment

## Overview

Acute malnutrition is a leading killer of children in crisis zones, contributing to approximately 45% of deaths among children under 5. It develops when food supply is disrupted, during displacement, or alongside infections that drain the body's resources. Early detection through simple screening saves lives - many cases can be treated in the community with ready-to-use therapeutic food (RUTF) without hospitalization.

## Understanding the Types

**Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM):**
- The most dangerous form - high risk of death without treatment
- MUAC (mid-upper arm circumference) less than 115 mm (11.5 cm) in children 6-59 months
- Visible severe wasting: ribs, spine, and shoulder bones clearly visible
- Nutritional edema: puffy swelling of feet, legs, hands, or face (kwashiorkor)
- Weight-for-height below -3 standard deviations

**Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM):**
- Serious but less immediately life-threatening
- MUAC between 115-124 mm (11.5-12.4 cm) in children 6-59 months
- Visible wasting but less severe
- Weight-for-height between -2 and -3 standard deviations

**Normal Range:**
- MUAC 125 mm (12.5 cm) or above in children 6-59 months
- Green zone on color-coded MUAC tapes

## MUAC Screening - How to Do It

MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference) is the simplest, fastest way to identify malnutrition. Anyone can learn to do it.

**Equipment:** MUAC tape (color-coded measuring tape) or any flexible measuring tape/string

**Procedure:**
1. Find the **left arm** of the child (by convention)
2. Find the **midpoint** of the upper arm: measure from the shoulder tip to the elbow tip, mark the halfway point
3. Wrap the MUAC tape around the arm at this midpoint - snug but not tight (should not compress the skin)
4. Read the measurement in millimeters

**Color-Coded Interpretation (standard MUAC tapes):**
- **GREEN (125+ mm):** Normal - no acute malnutrition
- **YELLOW (115-124 mm):** Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM) - needs supplementary feeding
- **RED (below 115 mm):** Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) - needs urgent treatment

**For Adults:** MUAC below 230 mm (23 cm) indicates acute malnutrition. Below 210 mm (21 cm) indicates severe acute malnutrition.

## Recognizing Malnutrition Without MUAC Tape

**Visual Signs of SAM:**
- Child looks like "skin and bones" - severe muscle wasting
- Old person's face on a child's body (loss of facial fat)
- Ribs, spine, shoulder blades clearly visible
- Skin hangs loose, especially on buttocks ("baggy pants" sign)
- Hair becomes thin, sparse, or changes color (reddish-brown)
- Child is listless, irritable, or does not cry

**Edematous Malnutrition (Kwashiorkor):**
- Swelling (edema) starting in the feet and legs, may progress to hands and face
- Child may NOT look thin due to swelling
- Skin may peel or crack, especially in skin folds
- Hair changes color and pulls out easily

**Check for Edema:** Press your thumb firmly on the top of each foot for 3 seconds. If a dent (pit) remains after releasing, edema is present.

## Immediate Steps (First 5 Minutes)

1. **Assess for Danger Signs** (see below) - these require immediate medical referral
2. **If child is conscious and able to eat:** Offer small amounts of food or therapeutic food immediately
3. **If child is breastfed:** Continue breastfeeding - never stop. Breastmilk is the safest food.
4. **Keep warm:** Malnourished children lose body heat very quickly. Wrap in blankets, keep skin-to-skin contact with mother.
5. **Treat/prevent low blood sugar:** If the child is lethargic or unconscious, give sugar water immediately (4 teaspoons sugar in half cup of water) - a few sips can be life-saving.

## Danger Signs Requiring Immediate Hospital Referral

A child with SAM PLUS any of these signs needs inpatient care - do not attempt to treat in the community:

- **Unable to eat or drink** (too weak to swallow, refuses all food)
- **Persistent vomiting** (vomits everything given)
- **Severe dehydration** (sunken eyes, skin pinch stays tented, no urine)
- **High fever** (above 38.5°C / 101°F) or hypothermia (below 35°C / 95°F)
- **Severe infection** (pneumonia, skin infection, urinary infection)
- **Unconscious or extremely lethargic** (does not respond to stimulation)
- **Severe edema** (swelling of both feet AND hands AND face)
- **Extensive skin lesions** (open, oozing skin breakdown)

## Ongoing Care - Therapeutic Feeding

### Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) - If Available
RUTF (such as Plumpy'Nut) is the WHO-recommended treatment for SAM without complications:
- Energy-dense, fortified peanut-based paste in sealed packets
- Does not require water, refrigeration, or cooking
- Child eats directly from the packet
- Typical dose: 150-200 kcal per kg of body weight per day
- Continue breastfeeding alongside RUTF
- Treatment typically lasts 6-8 weeks

### When RUTF Is Not Available
Provide the most energy-dense, nutrient-rich foods you can find:
- **Priority foods:** Peanut butter, full-fat milk, eggs, mashed beans, mashed bananas, porridge with added oil or butter
- **Add fat/oil** to all foods to increase calories (vegetable oil, butter, ghee)
- **Feed frequently:** At least 6-8 small meals per day - malnourished children cannot eat large amounts at once
- **Mash or soften** all food - weakened children cannot chew well
- **Add sugar** to porridge or milk for extra energy

### Feeding Schedule for Severely Malnourished Children
- **Phase 1 (Days 1-7 - Stabilization):** Small, frequent feeds every 2-3 hours, including through the night. Start slowly to avoid refeeding complications.
- **Phase 2 (After stabilization):** Gradually increase amounts. Child's appetite should increase as they recover.
- **Recovery phase:** Continue therapeutic feeding until MUAC is above 125 mm for two consecutive visits (usually weeks apart).

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT give large meals** to a severely malnourished child suddenly - this can cause "refeeding syndrome" (dangerous shifts in body chemistry that can cause heart failure). Start with small, frequent feeds.
- **Do NOT give high-protein supplements or IV glucose** without medical training - refeeding syndrome risk.
- **Do NOT stop breastfeeding** - breast milk is always appropriate even when the child is malnourished.
- **Do NOT give iron supplements** in the first week of SAM treatment - iron feeds infections and can be dangerous. Iron is added later after the child stabilizes.
- **Do NOT force-feed** - offer food patiently and frequently. Forced feeding can cause choking or aspiration.
- **Do NOT assume a swollen child is well-fed** - edematous malnutrition (kwashiorkor) makes children look puffy, not thin.
- **Do NOT ignore the mother's nutrition** - a malnourished mother cannot produce adequate breast milk. Feed the mother too.

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

- Any child with MUAC below 115 mm (red zone)
- Any child with edema of both feet
- Any SAM child with danger signs listed above
- Child not improving after 1 week of therapeutic feeding
- Weight loss during treatment
- Development of new edema during treatment
- Any adult with MUAC below 210 mm who is too weak to eat

## Prevention in Crisis Settings

- **Prioritize children 6-59 months and pregnant/breastfeeding women** for food distributions
- **Continue breastfeeding** exclusively for first 6 months, with complementary foods after 6 months
- **Screen regularly:** Measure MUAC of all children weekly during food crises
- **Treat infections promptly:** Diarrhea, measles, and respiratory infections rapidly worsen nutritional status
- **Vitamin A supplementation:** Reduces mortality in malnourished children. Given during measles vaccination campaigns.
- **Clean water and sanitation:** Prevents the diarrheal diseases that drive malnutrition

Sources: WHO Guidelines for Management of SAM, UNICEF/WHO MUAC Standards, WHO Community-Based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM) protocols.
