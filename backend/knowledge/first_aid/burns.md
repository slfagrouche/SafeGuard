# First Aid: Burns Treatment

## Overview

Burns damage the skin and underlying tissues through heat, chemicals, electricity, or radiation. Proper immediate treatment reduces pain, prevents infection, and minimizes scarring. The first 20 minutes of care are critical for limiting burn severity.

## Immediate Burn Treatment

**STOP the Burning Process:**
- Remove person from heat source immediately
- If clothing is on fire: STOP, DROP, and ROLL
- Remove burning or hot clothing (unless stuck to skin)
- Remove jewelry and tight items before swelling occurs
- For chemical burns: brush off dry chemicals first, then flush

**COOL the Burn:**
1. Place burned area under cool (not cold) running water for 20 minutes
   - Start cooling as soon as possible, ideally within seconds
   - 20 minutes minimum - this is critical for limiting tissue damage
   - Water temperature: Cool tap water, NOT ice water
   - Alternative if running water unavailable: cool, clean wet cloths (change frequently)

2. **Do NOT Use:**
   - Ice or ice water (causes additional tissue damage and hypothermia)
   - Butter, oil, grease, or petroleum jelly (traps heat, worsens burn)
   - Lotions, ointments, or creams during initial cooling
   - Cotton balls or fluffy materials (fibers stick to wound)

3. **After Cooling:**
   - Gently pat dry with clean cloth
   - Cover with sterile, non-stick dressing or clean cloth
   - Can apply thin layer of antibiotic ointment if available
   - Wrap loosely with gauze bandage

## Burn Severity Classification

**First-Degree Burns (Superficial):**
- **Appearance:** Red, no blisters, dry, painful
- **Depth:** Affects only outer skin layer (epidermis)
- **Examples:** Most sunburns, brief contact with hot objects
- **Healing:** 3-7 days without scarring
- **Treatment:** Cool water, moisturizer, over-the-counter pain relief
- **Seek Medical Care:** Usually not necessary unless very large area

**Second-Degree Burns (Partial Thickness):**
- **Appearance:** Deep red or splotchy, blisters (may appear within hours), moist, swollen, very painful
- **Depth:** Extends through outer layer into dermis
- **Examples:** Severe sunburn, scalds from boiling water, brief flame contact
- **Healing:** 2-3 weeks, may scar depending on depth
- **Treatment:** Cool water 20 min, protect blisters, sterile dressing, pain management
- **Seek Medical Care:** If larger than 3 inches (7.5 cm), on face/hands/feet/genitals/joints, or very painful

**Third-Degree Burns (Full Thickness):**
- **Appearance:** White, brown, or charred black, dry/leathery, may have no pain (nerve damage)
- **Depth:** Destroys all skin layers, may damage muscle and bone
- **Examples:** Prolonged flame exposure, electrical burns, chemical burns
- **Healing:** Requires surgery (skin grafts), severe scarring
- **Treatment:** Call emergency services immediately, do not remove stuck clothing, cool with water (if person not hypothermic), cover with clean dry cloth
- **Seek Medical Care:** IMMEDIATE emergency care required

## Blister Management

**DO NOT Pop Blisters:**
- Blisters are protective coverings for healing skin underneath
- Popping increases infection risk and pain
- Leave intact blisters alone

**If Blister Breaks on Its Own:**
- Gently wash area with clean water and mild soap
- Apply antibiotic ointment if available
- Cover with non-stick sterile dressing
- Change dressing daily or if wet/dirty
- Monitor for signs of infection

**Signs of Infection:**
- Increased pain, redness, or swelling
- Pus or cloudy fluid
- Red streaks extending from burn
- Fever over 100.4°F (38°C)
- Seek medical care immediately if infection suspected

## Special Burn Situations

**Chemical Burns:**
1. Safety first: Wear gloves, move person to safe area
2. Brush off any dry chemical powder before wetting
3. Remove contaminated clothing (do not pull over head - cut off)
4. Flush with large amounts of running water for at least 20 minutes (30+ minutes for strong acids/alkalis)
5. Do not attempt to neutralize (causes heat reaction, additional damage)
6. If chemical in eye: flush eye with water for 20+ minutes, seek immediate medical care
7. Seek medical attention for all chemical burns
8. Bring chemical container/information sheet to medical facility

**Electrical Burns:**
- Ensure power source is turned off before approaching victim
- Call emergency services immediately
- May have entry and exit wounds (exit often more severe)
- Internal damage may be extensive despite minor-appearing skin burns
- Monitor for cardiac arrest or breathing problems
- All electrical burns require emergency medical evaluation

**Radiation Burns:**
- Common from sun exposure or radiation therapy
- Treat as thermal burns with cool water
- Severe radiation burns (nuclear incidents) require specialized medical care
- Cover exposed areas and seek medical facility with radiation expertise

**Fire/Flame Burns:**
- Stop, drop, and roll if clothing on fire
- Do not run (increases flames)
- Cool immediately with water once flames out
- Large flame burns often require hospitalization
- Check for smoke inhalation (coughing, soot in nose/mouth, difficulty breathing)

## When to Seek Emergency Medical Care

**Call Emergency Services (103/101/112) Immediately If:**
- Burn is third-degree (white, charred, or painless)
- Burn covers large area (more than 10% of body)
- Burn is on face, hands, feet, genitals, or major joints
- Burn is from electrical source or chemicals
- Person is infant, elderly, or has chronic health conditions
- Burn circles an arm or leg (circumferential)
- Person shows signs of shock (rapid pulse, pale, confused)
- Smoke inhalation suspected (coughing, breathing difficulty)

**Seek Medical Care Within Hours If:**
- Second-degree burn larger than 3 inches
- Burn becomes infected (pus, increased redness, fever)
- Pain not controlled with over-the-counter medication
- Uncertainty about burn severity
- Burn caused by explosion (may have internal injuries)

## Ongoing Burn Care

**Dressing Changes:**
- Wash hands thoroughly before and after
- Clean burn gently with lukewarm water and mild soap
- Pat dry with clean cloth
- Apply thin layer antibiotic ointment if available
- Cover with non-stick sterile dressing
- Change dressing daily or when wet/dirty

**Pain Management:**
- Over-the-counter pain relievers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) per package instructions
- Cool compresses for 10-15 minutes several times daily
- Keep burn elevated if on limb (reduces swelling and pain)
- Avoid tight clothing over burn area

**Protecting Healing Burns:**
- Avoid direct sunlight on healing burn for at least one year
- Use sunscreen SPF 30+ on healed burns when in sun
- Moisturize healed burn area regularly to reduce itching and improve appearance
- Avoid re-injury to area while healing

**Scarring Prevention:**
- Keep burn moist during healing with ointment/moisturizer
- Protect from sun exposure
- Massage healed burn gently with moisturizer
- Silicone scar sheets may help (consult healthcare provider)
- Severe burns may require physical therapy to maintain range of motion

## What NOT to Do

**Never:**
- Apply ice directly to burns (causes frostbite and deeper damage)
- Use butter, oils, egg whites, or toothpaste (home remedies worsen burns)
- Pop blisters intentionally (increases infection risk)
- Peel away clothing stuck to burn (causes additional trauma)
- Use cotton balls or fluffy materials on open burn (fibers stick)
- Give food or drink to severely burned person (may need surgery)

## Prevention in Crisis Zones

**Cooking Safety:**
- Cook outside or in well-ventilated areas when possible
- Keep children away from cooking areas
- Secure pots so they cannot tip over
- Cool hot liquids before moving them

**Heating and Fire:**
- Maintain safe distance from open flames and heaters
- Never leave fires unattended
- Keep flammable materials away from heat sources
- Have exit plan in case of fire

Remember: Burns are very common in crisis zones due to cooking fires, damaged infrastructure, and inadequate safety measures. Quick action in the first 20 minutes significantly affects burn outcomes. When in doubt, seek medical evaluation.
