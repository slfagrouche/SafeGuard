# First Aid: Shock Recognition and Treatment

## Overview

Shock is a life-threatening medical emergency where the body's organs and tissues don't receive adequate blood flow and oxygen. It can develop from blood loss, severe infection, allergic reactions, trauma, or heart problems. Without treatment, shock rapidly becomes fatal. Early recognition and immediate treatment save lives.

## What Is Shock?

Shock occurs when the circulatory system fails to deliver enough oxygen-rich blood to the body's vital organs. This can result from:
- **Blood Loss:** Severe bleeding (most common in trauma)
- **Heart Problems:** Heart attack, heart failure
- **Fluid Loss:** Severe dehydration, burns, vomiting/diarrhea
- **Severe Infection:** Sepsis
- **Allergic Reaction:** Anaphylaxis
- **Spinal Injury:** Damage to nervous system control of blood vessels
- **Psychological:** Severe emotional stress (usually less severe)

## Recognizing Shock - Early Signs

**Early identification is critical. Look for these signs:**

**Skin Changes:**
- Pale, cool, clammy (cold sweat) skin
- Grayish or bluish tinge to lips and nails (cyanosis)
- Delayed capillary refill (press fingernail - color should return in 2 seconds)

**Vital Signs:**
- Rapid, weak pulse (over 100 beats/minute, hard to feel)
- Rapid, shallow breathing (over 20 breaths/minute)
- Low blood pressure (if equipment available to measure)

**Mental State:**
- Anxiety, restlessness, agitation
- Feeling of impending doom
- Confusion or disorientation
- Decreased alertness

**Other Signs:**
- Nausea or vomiting
- Excessive thirst
- Dizziness or weakness
- Dilated pupils

## Late Signs of Shock (Critical - Life-Threatening)

**If these appear, death may be imminent without immediate medical intervention:**
- Severe confusion or complete unresponsiveness
- Very weak, thready, or absent pulse
- Gasping, irregular breathing or stopped breathing
- Extremely pale or blue skin
- No urine output
- Eyes that appear glassy or stare vacantly

## Immediate Treatment for Shock

**Call for Emergency Medical Help First** - Shock requires professional medical care

**While Waiting for Help:**

1. **Position the Person:**
   - Lay person flat on their back on blanket or coat if available
   - Elevate legs 12 inches (30 cm) above heart level using backpack, rolled blankets, or similar
   - Exception - Do NOT elevate legs if:
     * Suspected head, neck, back, or leg injury
     * Elevating causes pain or breathing difficulty
     * Vomiting or likely to vomit (risk of choking)
     * Suspected heart attack or stroke
   
2. **Keep Warm (Prevent Heat Loss):**
   - Cover with blankets, coats, or any available material
   - Place blanket underneath person if on cold ground
   - Do NOT overheat - goal is maintain normal temperature, not warm up
   - Avoid hot water bottles or heating pads

3. **Do NOT Give Food or Drink:**
   - Nothing by mouth - no water, no food
   - May need emergency surgery where anesthesia required
   - Liquids may cause vomiting leading to choking
   - Moistening lips with water on cloth is acceptable if very thirsty

4. **Control Any Bleeding:**
   - Apply direct pressure to any visible bleeding wounds
   - Use tourniquets if necessary for life-threatening limb bleeding
   - Refer to bleeding control procedures

5. **Provide Reassurance:**
   - Speak calmly and reassuringly
   - Explain that help is coming
   - Physical presence and touch (holding hand) provides comfort
   - Anxiety worsens shock - calming helps

6. **Monitor Constantly:**
   - Check breathing every 2 minutes
   - Check pulse every 2 minutes
   - Watch for vomiting (turn head to side if occurs)
   - Be prepared to start CPR if breathing stops
   - Note any changes to report to medical responders

7. **Loosen Tight Clothing:**
   - Loosen belt, collar, tight waistbands
   - Do not move or remove clothing if this causes pain
   - Exception: If clothing is causing constriction that impairs circulation

## Special Types of Shock

### Anaphylactic Shock (Severe Allergic Reaction)

**Causes:** Insect stings, medications, foods (nuts, shellfish), latex

**Specific Signs:**
- Rapid onset (minutes after exposure)
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing, tight throat
- Swelling of face, lips, tongue
- Hives or widespread rash
- Abdominal cramping, nausea, vomiting
- Sense of impending doom

**Treatment:**
1. Call emergency services immediately
2. If person has epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen): 
   - Remove from carrying case
   - Pull off blue safety cap
   - Place orange tip against outer thigh (can go through clothing)
   - Push down hard until click heard and hold for 3 seconds
   - Remove and massage injection site
   - Keep person lying down with legs elevated
   - Second dose may be needed after 5-15 minutes if no improvement
3. If no improvement and no epinephrine available, CPR may be needed
4. Follow general shock treatment procedures

### Septic Shock (Severe Infection)

**Causes:** Untreated or severe bacterial infection spreading through bloodstream

**Specific Signs:**
- High fever initially (may progress to low temperature)
- Confusion or altered mental state
- Rapid breathing and heart rate
- Decreased urine output
- Skin may be warm and flushed initially, then cold and pale

**Treatment:**
- Requires immediate hospital care with IV antibiotics
- Follow general shock procedures
- Keep person comfortable and warm
- Transport to hospital urgently

### Cardiogenic Shock (Heart Problem)

**Causes:** Heart attack, severe heart failure

**Specific Signs:**
- Chest pain or pressure
- Shortness of breath
- Irregular heartbeat
- Confusion
- May have swelling in legs

**Treatment:**
- Call emergency services immediately
- If person has prescribed nitroglycerin for heart condition, assist with taking it
- Do NOT elevate legs (worsens heart workload)
- Position sitting up or semi-reclined if breathing easier
- Prepare for possible cardiac arrest and need for CPR

## Shock in Children

**Children compensate for shock longer than adults but deteriorate rapidly when they decompensate**

**Special Considerations:**
- Early signs may be subtle (irritability, lethargy)
- Rapid breathing is more sensitive indicator in children
- Skin color changes critical to watch
- Smaller blood loss causes shock (adjust expectations)
- Keep calm - your anxiety transfers to child
- Keep parent/caregiver with child if possible for comfort

## Monitoring While Waiting for Help

**Create monitoring log if possible - note time and findings:**

**Every 2 Minutes:**
- Is person breathing? How fast?
- Can you feel pulse? How fast? Strong or weak?
- Is person conscious? Can they respond to questions?
- Skin color - any changes?

**Signs of Improvement:**
- Pulse slows and becomes stronger
- Breathing slows and deepens
- Skin color improves, becomes warmer
- Person becomes more alert

**Signs of Deterioration:**
- Pulse becomes weaker, faster, or disappears
- Breathing becomes irregular or stops
- Consciousness decreases
- Skin becomes paler or more blue
- Prepare to start CPR

## When Emergency Care Is Required

**All shock situations require emergency medical care. Call immediately.**

Shock cannot be fully treated in the field. Person needs:
- IV fluids to restore blood volume
- Medications to support blood pressure
- Oxygen supplementation
- Treatment of underlying cause (surgery for bleeding, antibiotics for infection, etc.)
- Advanced monitoring and life support if needed

**While waiting, your goals are:**
- Prevent condition from worsening
- Maintain breathing and circulation
- Provide comfort and reassurance
- Keep person alive until professional help arrives

## After Recovery

**Follow-Up Care:**
- Even if person appears to recover, medical evaluation is essential
- Shock indicates serious underlying problem requiring diagnosis and treatment
- May have organ damage from decreased blood flow
- Could deteriorate again without warning

**Prevent Future Shock:**
- Identify and treat underlying cause
- If allergies caused anaphylactic shock, carry epinephrine always
- Medical alert bracelet for known severe allergies
- Teach family members how to use epinephrine auto-injector

## Prevention in Crisis Zones

**Reducing Shock Risk:**
- Prompt control of any significant bleeding
- Stay hydrated (shock from dehydration common in crisis situations)
- Treat infections early before they become severe
- Protect from extreme temperatures
- Recognize early signs of shock in trauma victims
- Have basic medical supplies including trauma dressings

Remember: Shock is a medical emergency requiring immediate action and professional care. Your rapid recognition and appropriate first aid treatment can save a life, but shock victims always need hospital care. Do not wait to see if they improve - call for help immediately and provide supportive care while waiting.
