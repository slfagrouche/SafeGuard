# First Aid: Mental Health Crisis in Conflict Zones

## Overview

Mental health injuries are real injuries. In conflict-affected populations, approximately 1 in 5 people (22%) will develop depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, or schizophrenia. Most people in crisis zones experience psychological distress - anxiety, sadness, sleeplessness, irritability, and hopelessness. For most, this distress improves over time with support. For some, it becomes a lasting condition that needs care. You do not need to be a mental health professional to provide effective psychological first aid. Your calm presence and practical help can make a significant difference.

## Recognizing Psychological Distress

### Normal Stress Reactions (Expected After Crisis Events)
These are NORMAL responses to abnormal situations. Most people experience some of these:
- Difficulty sleeping, nightmares
- Anxiety, feeling on edge, hypervigilance (constantly scanning for danger)
- Sadness, crying, feeling numb
- Irritability, anger outbursts
- Difficulty concentrating
- Physical symptoms: headaches, stomach pain, muscle tension
- Wanting to be alone OR not wanting to be alone
- Loss of appetite or overeating

**These typically improve over days to weeks** with safety, social support, and basic needs being met.

### Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) - When to Be Concerned
PTSD may develop weeks or months after a traumatic event. Look for symptoms lasting MORE than one month:

**Re-experiencing:**
- Flashbacks (feeling like the event is happening again)
- Vivid nightmares about the event
- Intense distress when reminded of the event (sounds, smells, sights)
- Physical reactions to reminders (racing heart, sweating, trembling)

**Avoidance:**
- Avoiding places, people, or activities that are reminders
- Not wanting to talk about what happened
- Emotional numbness, feeling detached from others
- Loss of interest in activities previously enjoyed

**Hyperarousal:**
- Constantly on alert, scanning for danger (cannot relax)
- Exaggerated startle response (jumping at sudden sounds)
- Difficulty sleeping (falling asleep or staying asleep)
- Irritability, anger, aggressive behavior
- Difficulty concentrating

**Negative Changes:**
- Persistent feelings of guilt, shame, or blame
- Loss of trust in others
- Feeling the world is completely dangerous
- Inability to experience positive emotions
- Memory problems related to the trauma

### Acute Crisis (Immediate Danger Signs)
Act immediately if someone shows:
- **Talking about wanting to die** or end their life
- **Self-harm** - cutting, burning, or injuring themselves
- **Complete withdrawal** - not eating, drinking, or responding to others for extended periods
- **Severe dissociation** - staring blankly, not responding, seeming "not present"
- **Psychotic symptoms** - hearing voices, seeing things others cannot, paranoid beliefs disconnected from reality
- **Extreme agitation** - threatening violence to others, destroying things, uncontrollable rage

## Psychological First Aid (PFA) - What Anyone Can Do

PFA is the WHO-recommended approach for helping people in crisis. It is NOT therapy and does NOT require professional training. It follows three principles: **Look, Listen, Link.**

### LOOK (Assess the Situation)
1. Is the person safe? Are there ongoing threats?
2. Are basic needs met? (water, food, shelter, medical care)
3. Is the person having a severe reaction (see acute crisis signs above)?
4. Are there vulnerable people? (children alone, elderly, people with disabilities)

### LISTEN (Provide Support)
1. **Approach calmly and respectfully** - introduce yourself, ask if you can help
2. **Ask about their needs** - "What do you need right now?" Focus on practical concerns first
3. **Listen actively:**
   - Give your full attention
   - Nod, make eye contact (culturally appropriate)
   - Do not interrupt
   - Accept silence - you do not need to fill every pause
   - Reflect what they say: "It sounds like you are feeling..."
4. **Validate their feelings** - "It makes sense that you feel this way given what happened"
5. **Do not pressure them to talk** about traumatic details. Let them share what THEY want to share, at their own pace

### LINK (Connect with Resources and Support)
1. Help with practical needs: food, water, shelter, medical care, information about family members
2. Connect with family or community - social support is the strongest protective factor
3. Provide information about available services (if they exist)
4. Help them identify their own coping strategies: "What has helped you through difficult times before?"

## Grounding Techniques (For Panic, Flashbacks, Dissociation)

When someone is overwhelmed by panic, having a flashback, or seems disconnected from reality, grounding brings them back to the present moment.

### 5-4-3-2-1 Technique
Guide the person through this, speaking calmly and slowly:

"Tell me:"
- **5 things you can SEE** right now (the sky, a wall, your hands, a tree, a stone)
- **4 things you can TOUCH** right now (the ground under your feet, your clothing, the air on your skin, a nearby object)
- **3 things you can HEAR** right now (wind, birds, voices, traffic, your own breathing)
- **2 things you can SMELL** right now (air, dust, food, fabric)
- **1 thing you can TASTE** right now (water, food, the inside of your mouth)

This works because it forces the brain to focus on the present reality rather than the traumatic memory.

### Breathing Technique (Box Breathing)
For panic, rapid breathing, or anxiety:

1. **Breathe IN** slowly for 4 counts (1... 2... 3... 4...)
2. **HOLD** for 4 counts (1... 2... 3... 4...)
3. **Breathe OUT** slowly for 4 counts (1... 2... 3... 4...)
4. **HOLD** for 4 counts (1... 2... 3... 4...)
5. Repeat 4-6 times

If the person is too distressed to count, breathe WITH them - exaggerate your own slow breathing so they can match your rhythm.

### Physical Grounding
- Press feet firmly into the ground and focus on the sensation
- Hold a cold object (water bottle, stone) and focus on the temperature
- Splash cold water on face or wrists
- Clench fists tightly for 5 seconds, then release - notice the difference
- Stamp feet or walk, focusing on each step

### Orientation Questions
If the person seems dissociated (staring, unresponsive, "not here"):
- "Can you tell me your name?"
- "Do you know where you are?"
- "Can you tell me today's date?" or "Is it day or night?"
- "Can you squeeze my hand?"

Speak calmly and clearly. Wait for a response. Repeat if needed.

## Helping Children

Children process trauma differently from adults. Common reactions by age:

**Young Children (Under 6):**
- Clinging to caregivers, not wanting to be separated
- Regression (bedwetting, thumb-sucking, baby talk)
- Repetitive play acting out the event
- New fears (darkness, monsters, being alone)
- Crying, screaming, tantrums

**Older Children (6-12):**
- Difficulty concentrating, school problems
- Withdrawal from friends or activities
- Complaints of headaches or stomachaches
- Nightmares, sleep problems
- Guilt ("I should have done something")
- Acting out (aggression, defiance)

**Adolescents:**
- Risk-taking behavior
- Withdrawal, isolation
- Anger, defiance
- Substance use
- Physical complaints
- Feelings of helplessness or hopelessness

### How to Help Children
1. **Reunite with caregivers** as the top priority - separation from parents is one of the most traumatic experiences for a child
2. **Keep routines** - any structure (meal times, sleep times, activities) provides security
3. **Allow play** - children process trauma through play. Do not suppress it, even if the play seems morbid (war games, pretending to die). This is normal processing.
4. **Be honest** in age-appropriate language - children sense lies and this destroys trust
5. **Physical comfort** - hugs, holding, physical closeness (with appropriate consent)
6. **Reassure safety** - "You are safe now. I am here with you."
7. **Limit exposure** to graphic images, adult conversations about violence, and news

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT say "I know how you feel"** - you do not, and it dismisses their experience. Say "I can see this is very difficult" instead.
- **Do NOT say "It could be worse" or "At least you're alive"** - this minimizes their pain
- **Do NOT say "You need to be strong" or "Don't cry"** - crying is a healthy response to terrible events
- **Do NOT force someone to talk** about what happened. Forced debriefing (making people recount traumatic events) has been shown to INCREASE PTSD rates.
- **Do NOT make promises you cannot keep** ("Everything will be okay," "You'll be home soon")
- **Do NOT give false reassurance** - be honest about what you know and do not know
- **Do NOT diagnose** - you are not a therapist. Do not say "You have PTSD" or "You are depressed"
- **Do NOT give medications** (sleeping pills, sedatives) without medical guidance
- **Do NOT share their story** with others without permission
- **Do NOT ignore your own stress** - helpers who are overwhelmed become less effective and can burn out
- **Do NOT use alcohol or drugs** as a coping mechanism, and do not offer them to others

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

**Immediately:**
- Talking about suicide or wanting to die
- Self-harm or suicide attempt
- Complete refusal to eat or drink for more than 24 hours
- Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, severe paranoia, disconnection from reality)
- Extreme agitation with risk of violence to self or others
- Severe dissociation (unresponsive for extended periods)

**Within Days:**
- Symptoms of PTSD lasting more than one month
- Inability to care for self or dependents (not eating, not caring for children)
- Substance abuse as coping mechanism
- Persistent sleep deprivation (not sleeping for days)
- Grief that prevents any daily functioning

**Ongoing Support Needed:**
- Anyone who has lost a family member to violence
- Sexual violence survivors (provide support, never pressure to share details)
- Children who have been separated from all caregivers
- People who witnessed mass casualties
- Former detained persons or torture survivors

## Self-Care for Helpers

If you are providing support to others in crisis, you are also at risk. Helpers who ignore their own needs burn out or develop secondary trauma.

**Basic Self-Care:**
- Eat and drink regularly, even when you do not feel hungry
- Sleep when you can - even short rest helps
- Talk to someone you trust about how YOU are feeling
- Take breaks from helping - even 10 minutes of quiet
- Limit your exposure to traumatic details when possible
- Maintain some form of routine or ritual (prayer, exercise, journaling)
- It is okay to cry, to feel angry, to feel helpless - these are normal responses
- Know your limits - you cannot help everyone, and that is not a failure

**Warning Signs of Helper Burnout:**
- Feeling numb or disconnected
- Irritability or anger at the people you are helping
- Difficulty sleeping or nightmares
- Using substances to cope
- Feeling hopeless or that nothing matters
- Physical exhaustion that does not improve with rest

If you notice these in yourself, step back and seek support. You help no one by destroying yourself.
