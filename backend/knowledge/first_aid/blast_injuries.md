# First Aid: Blast and Explosion Injuries

## Overview

Blast injuries from explosions (shelling, airstrikes, IEDs, landmines) are among the most common trauma in conflict zones. Explosions cause a unique combination of injuries through multiple mechanisms simultaneously. Understanding the different types of blast injury helps you prioritize treatment - some of the most dangerous injuries are invisible from the outside.

**Critical Concept:** After any explosion, expect the number of casualties presenting in the first hour to roughly double as less-injured survivors arrive. People between a blast and a building suffer 2-3 times worse injuries than those in open areas.

## Types of Blast Injuries

### Primary Blast Injury (Pressure Wave)
**Cause:** The invisible shockwave/overpressure from the explosion itself.

**Most Dangerous Because:** Causes severe internal injuries with NO visible external wounds. A person may look completely unharmed but have life-threatening organ damage.

**Organs Affected (in order of vulnerability):**
1. **Ears:** Most common - eardrum rupture causes sudden hearing loss, pain, bleeding from ear, ringing
2. **Lungs ("Blast Lung"):** Leading cause of death in initial survivors. Lungs are bruised and bleed internally.
3. **Intestines:** May not show symptoms for hours or days - delayed abdominal pain, bloody stool

**Signs of Blast Lung (Critical):**
- Difficulty breathing, shortness of breath
- Coughing, especially coughing blood
- Chest pain or tightness
- Blue lips or fingertips
- Can deteriorate rapidly from "fine" to critical

### Secondary Blast Injury (Shrapnel/Flying Debris)
**Cause:** Objects propelled by the explosion - shrapnel, glass, rubble, fragments.

**This causes the MOST casualties** because debris travels hundreds of meters.

**Treatment:** Treat as penetrating trauma - see bleeding control and embedded object protocols.

### Tertiary Blast Injury (Body Displacement)
**Cause:** The blast wind throws the person's body against objects (walls, ground, vehicles).

**Results in:** Fractures, head injuries, blunt trauma. Children are at higher risk due to lower body weight.

### Quaternary Blast Injury (Everything Else)
**Cause:** Burns from fireball, crush injuries from structural collapse, dust inhalation, chemical exposure.

**Also includes:** Post-traumatic stress, which can affect even physically uninjured survivors.

## Recognizing the Situation

**After any explosion, assess:**
- Is the area safe? Are there secondary devices or ongoing shelling? Do NOT rush in without checking.
- How many casualties? Call for help immediately if multiple.
- Is the person conscious? Breathing? Bleeding?

**Hidden Injuries to Watch For:**
- Person says "I'm fine" but was close to the blast - they may have primary blast injuries developing
- Hearing loss or ringing (tinnitus) after blast = ear damage, suggests possible lung damage too
- Abdominal pain developing hours after blast = possible intestinal injury

## Immediate Steps (First 5 Minutes)

### 1. Scene Safety
- **Check for secondary explosions** - in conflict zones, a second blast may be timed to hit rescuers
- Wait at least 60 seconds after an explosion before approaching if in an active conflict area
- Watch for unstable structures, fires, downed power lines
- Approach from upwind if smoke or dust is present

### 2. Triage (Multiple Casualties)
If there are multiple victims, you must prioritize. Use the START triage method:
- **Can they walk?** Walking wounded are lowest priority (treat last)
- **Are they breathing?** If not breathing after opening airway = deceased (move to next person)
- **Breathing rate?** Over 30 breaths/minute = immediate priority (RED)
- **Pulse present?** No radial pulse (wrist) = immediate priority (RED)
- **Can they follow commands?** Cannot follow simple commands = immediate priority (RED)

### 3. Treat Life Threats (MARCH Order)
For each casualty, address in this order:

**M - Massive Hemorrhage:**
- Apply tourniquet to any limb with life-threatening bleeding (bright red, spurting, or pooling blood)
- For non-limb bleeding: apply direct pressure, pack wounds (see bleeding guide)
- Traumatic amputations require immediate tourniquet above the amputation site

**A - Airway:**
- Open airway: head-tilt, chin-lift (or jaw thrust if spinal injury suspected)
- Clear visible debris from mouth
- Place unconscious breathing person in recovery position (on their side)

**R - Respiration:**
- Check for chest wounds - a sucking chest wound (air going in and out of chest wound) needs immediate sealing
- Seal open chest wound with any airtight material (plastic wrap, credit card, tape) on THREE sides, leaving one side open as a flutter valve
- If blast lung suspected: position sitting upright if possible, do not give excessive fluids

**C - Circulation:**
- Treat for shock: lay flat, elevate legs if no spinal injury suspected
- Keep warm with blankets, clothing, or body heat
- Monitor pulse and consciousness

**H - Hypothermia/Head injury:**
- Prevent heat loss (blast victims cool rapidly from blood loss and exposure)
- For head injuries: keep head elevated slightly, monitor consciousness

## Ongoing Care Until Help Arrives

1. **Monitor everyone** exposed to the blast, even those who seem fine. Primary blast injuries can worsen over hours.
2. **Reassess every 5 minutes:** Check breathing rate, pulse, consciousness level, and wound status.
3. **Watch for blast lung deterioration:** Increasing difficulty breathing, coughing blood, blue coloring.
4. **Do not remove embedded objects** (shrapnel, glass, metal fragments) - stabilize in place with padding.
5. **Keep wounds covered** to prevent infection - use the cleanest material available.
6. **Document:** Write injuries and treatment times on the person's body or clothing with marker if possible. This helps medical teams.
7. **Provide reassurance:** Talk to the person calmly. Fear and panic worsen shock.
8. **Watch for delayed abdominal symptoms:** Any person close to a blast who develops abdominal pain, nausea, or bloody stool in the hours after needs urgent medical care.

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT rush into a blast scene** without checking for secondary devices or ongoing threats
- **Do NOT assume someone is fine** because they have no visible injuries - primary blast injuries are internal
- **Do NOT remove embedded shrapnel or fragments** - they may be plugging blood vessels
- **Do NOT give blast lung patients excessive fluids** - their damaged lungs cannot handle extra fluid
- **Do NOT put positive-pressure ventilation** (blowing air into mouth) on suspected blast lung patients if they are breathing on their own - this can worsen lung tears
- **Do NOT straighten or reposition obviously broken limbs** unless circulation to the extremity is compromised (no pulse below the break)
- **Do NOT move patients with suspected spinal injuries** unless they are in immediate danger (fire, structural collapse)
- **Do NOT ignore psychological injury** - traumatic stress is a real injury that needs care

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

**Immediate (life-threatening):**
- Any life-threatening bleeding
- Difficulty breathing or coughing blood (blast lung)
- Unconsciousness or altered consciousness
- Signs of shock (rapid pulse, pale skin, confusion)
- Traumatic amputation
- Open chest or abdominal wounds
- Suspected spinal injury (neck or back pain after being thrown)

**Urgent (within hours):**
- Any person who was within 10 meters of an explosion, even if currently feeling fine
- Hearing loss or persistent ringing after blast
- Developing abdominal pain after blast
- Worsening headache, confusion, or vision changes after blast
- Any embedded foreign objects

**Delayed (within days):**
- Persistent hearing problems
- Worsening pain at any site
- Signs of wound infection (redness, warmth, pus, fever)
- Psychological symptoms (flashbacks, severe anxiety, inability to sleep)

## Special Considerations for Conflict Zones

- **Eardrum rupture** from blast is extremely common. If someone cannot hear you, write instructions down or use gestures.
- **Dust inhalation** after building collapse: cover mouth and nose with wet cloth if possible.
- **Crush injuries** from collapsed structures: see the Fractures and Crush Injuries guide before releasing trapped persons.
- **Burns** from blast fireball: see the Burns guide for treatment.
- First symptoms of blast-induced brain injury may not appear for weeks or months. Encourage follow-up care.
