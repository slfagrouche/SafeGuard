# First Aid: Severe Bleeding Control

## Overview

Severe bleeding is one of the most time-critical medical emergencies. An adult can lose consciousness from blood loss in 3-5 minutes and die within 10-15 minutes from uncontrolled arterial bleeding. Rapid intervention saves lives.

## Direct Pressure Technique (Primary Method)

**When to Use:** ALL bleeding situations - this is your first action

**Procedure:**
1. **Personal Safety First:** Wear gloves if available (improvise with plastic bags). Avoid direct contact with blood when possible.

2. **Apply Pressure Immediately:**
   - Place clean cloth, gauze, or clothing directly over the wound
   - Apply firm, continuous pressure with flat of your hand or fingers
   - Do not remove cloth if it becomes soaked - add more layers on top
   - Maintain pressure for minimum 15 minutes without checking

3. **Position:**
   - If possible, elevate injured limb above heart level (reduces blood flow to area)
   - Have person lie down to prevent shock
   - Exception: Do not elevate if fracture suspected until immobilized

4. **Pressure Point Technique (Additional):**
   - For limb bleeding: press on artery between wound and heart
   - Arm: brachial artery (inside upper arm)
   - Leg: femoral artery (groin crease)
   - Use this in addition to direct pressure, not instead of it

## Tourniquet Application (Life-Threatening Limb Bleeding Only)

**When to Use:**
- Arterial bleeding from arm or leg that cannot be controlled with direct pressure
- Multiple casualties requiring triage (tourniquet allows moving to next patient)
- Amputation or partial amputation
- Situations where maintaining direct pressure is impossible

**CRITICAL:** Tourniquets are for life-threatening limb bleeding only. Improper use can result in limb loss.

**Commercial Tourniquet (CAT, SOFT-T, others):**
1. Apply tourniquet 2-3 inches (5-7 cm) above wound (between wound and heart)
2. Apply over single bone if possible (upper arm or upper thigh ideal)
3. Do not apply over joint
4. Tighten windlass rod until bleeding completely stops
5. Secure windlass with time strap
6. Write time of application on tourniquet (or person's forehead) with marker: "TK 14:30"
7. Do not loosen or remove - leave for medical professionals

**Improvised Tourniquet:**
1. Use at least 2 inches (5 cm) wide material (belt, torn cloth, scarf)
2. Never use wire, rope, or narrow materials (causes tissue damage)
3. Wrap around limb 2-3 inches above wound
4. Tie a half-knot, place a stick or rod on knot, tie full knot over stick
5. Twist stick to tighten until bleeding stops
6. Secure stick so it cannot unwind
7. Mark time of application
8. Do not remove

**Tourniquet Time Limits:**
- Under 2 hours: Minimal risk of permanent damage
- 2-4 hours: Increasing risk, limb often salvageable
- Over 4 hours: Significant risk of limb loss
- Get to medical care as quickly as possible

## Wound Packing (Deep Wounds)

**When to Use:**
- Deep wounds where direct pressure alone is insufficient
- Stab wounds, gunshot wounds
- Junctional areas where tourniquet cannot be applied (groin, armpit, neck base)

**Procedure:**
1. **Control the Situation:**
   - Expose wound fully by cutting away clothing
   - Identify source of bleeding inside wound

2. **Pack the Wound:**
   - Use gauze roll, clean cloth, or hemostatic gauze (preferred if available)
   - Insert gauze directly into wound, pushing toward bleeding source
   - Pack tightly and firmly - this will be uncomfortable for patient
   - Fill entire wound cavity with packing material
   - Continue until wound is tightly packed and bulging slightly

3. **Apply Pressure:**
   - Once packed, apply firm direct pressure over packed wound for 3-5 minutes
   - Then apply pressure dressing to maintain pressure
   - Monitor for continued bleeding

**Hemostatic Agents (QuikClot, Celox, etc.):**
- Specially designed gauze or granules that accelerate clotting
- Follow package instructions if available
- Still requires wound packing and pressure
- Significantly more effective than plain gauze

## Recognizing Severe Blood Loss (Shock)

**Early Signs:**
- Rapid pulse (over 100 beats/minute)
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Increased breathing rate
- Anxiety, restlessness

**Late Signs (Life-Threatening):**
- Confusion, decreased consciousness
- Weak or absent pulse
- Very pale or bluish skin
- Gasping breathing
- Unresponsive

**Treating for Shock:**
- Lay person flat (unless vomiting or breathing difficulty)
- Elevate legs 12 inches (30 cm) if no spinal injury suspected
- Keep warm with blankets (prevent heat loss, do not overheat)
- Do not give food or water
- Constantly monitor breathing and pulse
- Provide reassurance

## When to Seek Emergency Medical Care

**Immediate Medical Attention Required:**
- Any arterial bleeding (bright red, spurting blood)
- Bleeding that does not stop after 15 minutes of direct pressure
- Wound over 1 inch (2.5 cm) deep or with visible fat/muscle
- Any sign of shock (rapid pulse, pale skin, confusion)
- Large amount of blood loss (more than 1 cup)
- Bleeding from head, chest, or abdomen
- Any wound requiring tourniquet or wound packing

**While Waiting for Help:**
- Maintain pressure on wound
- Monitor person's condition every 2-3 minutes
- Keep person warm and lying down
- Document time treatments applied
- Do not give food or water

## Specific Bleeding Situations

**Head Wounds:**
- Scalp bleeds heavily due to rich blood supply
- Apply firm pressure with palm over wound
- Do not apply pressure if skull feels soft or fragmented (skull fracture)
- If skull fracture suspected, apply light pressure around edges only

**Nosebleeds:**
- Sit upright, lean slightly forward (prevents blood going down throat)
- Pinch soft part of nose firmly for 10-15 minutes continuously
- Apply cold compress to bridge of nose
- Do not tilt head back, do not pack nostril unless trained

**Internal Bleeding (Suspected):**
- Signs: Bruising, rigid or distended abdomen, coughing/vomiting blood, confusion
- Action: Immediate medical care, treat for shock, monitor closely
- Cannot be controlled with direct pressure

**Embedded Objects:**
- Do NOT remove objects embedded in wound (knives, shrapnel, rebar, etc.)
- Removal may worsen bleeding or cause additional damage
- Stabilize object in place with bulky dressings around it
- Apply pressure around object without pushing on it
- Immediate medical attention required

## Prevention of Disease Transmission

**Protect Yourself:**
- Use gloves, plastic bags, or multiple layers of cloth as barrier
- Avoid touching your face during treatment
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after treatment
- Clean any blood exposure on your skin immediately

**Clean the Wound (Only Minor Bleeding):**
- For minor cuts: rinse with clean water once bleeding controlled
- For severe bleeding: Control bleeding first, cleaning is secondary
- Do not waste time cleaning life-threatening bleeding

Remember: The most important actions for severe bleeding are direct pressure and calling for help. Every second counts. Act quickly and maintain pressure until help arrives or bleeding completely stops.
