# First Aid: Hypothermia and Heat Stroke

## Overview

Temperature-related emergencies are common in crisis zones. Displaced people often lack adequate shelter, clothing, or water. Hypothermia kills people sleeping rough in winter, in flooded areas, or after blood loss. Heat stroke kills during summer evacuations, in overcrowded shelters without ventilation, and during long walks to safety. Both are treatable if caught early, and both are fatal if ignored.

---

## HYPOTHERMIA

### What Is Hypothermia?
The body's core temperature drops below 35°C (95°F). The body loses heat faster than it can produce it. In crisis zones, hypothermia is common from exposure to cold weather, wet clothing, wind, blood loss (which reduces the body's ability to generate heat), and lack of shelter.

### Recognizing Hypothermia by Stage

**Stage 1 - Mild (32-35°C / 89-95°F): CONSCIOUS AND SHIVERING**
- Uncontrollable shivering (the body's attempt to generate heat)
- Cold, pale skin
- Fumbling hands, difficulty with fine movements (cannot button clothing)
- Increased urination
- Confusion beginning, poor decision-making
- Goosebumps, slightly slurred speech

**Stage 2 - Moderate (28-32°C / 82-89°F): DROWSY, SHIVERING STOPS**
- **Shivering stops** - this is a DANGER SIGN, not improvement. The body has given up trying to warm itself.
- Increasing confusion and drowsiness
- Slurred speech becomes pronounced
- Slow, shallow breathing
- Weak pulse
- Loss of coordination, stumbling
- Irrational behavior (may resist help)

**Stage 3 - Severe (20-28°C / 68-82°F): UNCONSCIOUS**
- Unconsciousness
- Muscle rigidity (body feels stiff)
- Very slow or irregular heartbeat
- Fixed, dilated pupils
- Very slow or absent breathing
- May appear dead but MAY still be alive

**Paradoxical Undressing (WARNING):** In 20-50% of severe hypothermia cases, the person removes their own clothing despite being dangerously cold. This is a brain malfunction, not a sign they are warm. If you find someone undressed in the cold, suspect severe hypothermia.

### Immediate Steps for Hypothermia (First 5 Minutes)

1. **Move to shelter.** Get out of the cold, wind, and wet. Any shelter is better than none - a car, a building, a tarp, behind a wall.
2. **Remove wet clothing.** Wet fabric conducts heat away from the body 25 times faster than dry air. Replace with dry clothing or blankets.
3. **Insulate from the ground.** The ground steals heat rapidly. Place blankets, cardboard, backpacks, or any insulation between the person and the ground.
4. **Cover the head and neck.** Significant heat is lost from the head. Use a hat, scarf, or cloth.
5. **Assess severity** - is the person shivering (mild) or not shivering (moderate/severe)?

### Treatment by Severity

**Mild Hypothermia (Still Shivering and Alert):**
- Give warm, sweet drinks (tea, soup, sugar water) - NOT alcohol, NOT coffee
- Encourage gentle voluntary movement (walking, arm swinging) to generate heat
- Apply warm blankets and layers
- Use body heat: another person lies close, skin-to-skin contact under blankets
- Recovery usually occurs with warming over 30-60 minutes

**Moderate Hypothermia (Drowsy, Not Shivering):**
- **Handle VERY gently.** Rough handling can trigger fatal heart rhythm (ventricular fibrillation).
- Do NOT shake, rub, or move the person vigorously
- Apply external heat sources to core areas: warm water bottles or warm (not hot) compresses to armpits, groin, and neck
- Wrap in blankets, including the head
- If conscious enough to swallow safely: warm sweet drinks in small sips
- Do NOT give drinks if drowsy or confused (choking risk)
- This person needs medical care

**Severe Hypothermia (Unconscious):**
- **Check for breathing for a full 60 seconds** - breathing may be extremely slow (2-3 breaths per minute)
- If no breathing detected after 60 seconds: begin CPR
- Handle with extreme gentleness
- Insulate and warm passively (blankets, remove from cold)
- If available: warm IV fluids
- **"You are not dead until you are warm and dead"** - people have survived extreme hypothermia. Continue CPR and warming until medical help arrives.
- This is a medical emergency - evacuate to hospital

### Ongoing Care
1. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously
2. Continue warming gradually - do not try to warm rapidly
3. Keep in a horizontal position if possible (prevents blood pressure drop)
4. Re-check temperature if a thermometer is available
5. Watch for "afterdrop" - core temperature may continue falling briefly even after warming begins. This is normal; continue warming efforts.

## HEAT STROKE

### What Is Heat Stroke?
The body's temperature rises above 40°C (104°F) and the body's cooling mechanisms fail. This is a TRUE MEDICAL EMERGENCY with up to 50-65% mortality in severe cases. Heat stroke is different from heat exhaustion (which is the stage before) - heat stroke means the body can no longer cool itself.

### Heat Exhaustion vs. Heat Stroke

**Heat Exhaustion (Precursor - Act Now to Prevent Heat Stroke):**
- Heavy sweating
- Weakness, fatigue, dizziness
- Nausea, possible vomiting
- Cool, pale, clammy skin
- Fast, weak pulse
- Muscle cramps
- Headache
- The person CAN still sweat and is usually conscious

**Heat Stroke (Emergency):**
- Body temperature above 40°C / 104°F
- **Classic heat stroke:** Hot, RED, DRY skin (sweating has stopped - body's cooling has failed)
- **Exertional heat stroke:** May still be sweating (during physical exertion) but body temperature is dangerously high
- Confusion, bizarre behavior, delirium, aggression
- Rapid, strong pulse (then may become weak)
- Throbbing headache
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness
- Nausea and vomiting

### Immediate Steps for Heat Stroke (First 5 Minutes)

**This is a race against time. Every minute of overheating causes more organ damage.**

1. **Move to shade or coolest available area** immediately
2. **Remove excess clothing** - strip down to minimal clothing
3. **Begin aggressive cooling immediately:**
   - **Best method:** Cold water immersion - submerge as much of the body as possible in any available cold water (river, tank, tub, large basin)
   - **If immersion not possible:** Spray or pour water over the entire body and fan vigorously (evaporation is very effective)
   - Place ice packs or cold wet cloths on neck, armpits, and groin (major blood vessels close to the skin)
   - Pour cold water over the head
4. **Do NOT give anything by mouth** if the person is confused or unconscious
5. **If conscious and alert:** Give cool (not ice-cold) water to drink in small sips

### Ongoing Care Until Help Arrives

1. **Continue cooling** until body feels cooler to touch or the person becomes more alert
2. **Monitor consciousness** - confusion clearing up is a good sign
3. **Position:** If conscious, sitting up or recovery position. If unconscious, recovery position (on side).
4. **Do NOT stop cooling** even if the person says they feel cold or shivers. Continue until body temperature is clearly dropping.
5. **Monitor for seizures** - if they occur, protect the head, do not restrain, do not put anything in the mouth
6. **Watch for vomiting** - turn to side to prevent choking

### Heat Exhaustion Treatment (Prevent Progression to Heat Stroke)
- Move to cool shade
- Remove excess clothing, loosen tight clothing
- Give cool water or ORS to drink (small, frequent sips)
- Fan the person, apply cool wet cloths
- Elevate legs slightly
- Rest in a cool place for several hours
- If symptoms worsen or do not improve in 30 minutes, treat as heat stroke

## What NOT to Do

**Hypothermia:**
- **Do NOT give alcohol** - it makes you FEEL warm but actually accelerates heat loss by dilating blood vessels
- **Do NOT put the person in a hot bath** or use very hot water bottles - rapid external heating can cause dangerous heart rhythms and blood pressure drops
- **Do NOT rub or massage the limbs vigorously** - this pushes cold blood from extremities to the core, worsening core cooling
- **Do NOT let the person walk or exert themselves** if moderate/severe hypothermia - exertion can trigger cardiac arrest
- **Do NOT use heating pads directly on skin** - hypothermic people cannot feel burns
- **Do NOT assume someone is dead** in severe hypothermia - vital signs may be undetectable but the person may still be alive. Warm first, then assess.

**Heat Stroke:**
- **Do NOT give aspirin or acetaminophen/paracetamol** for heat stroke fever - these medications do not work for heat stroke and can worsen liver damage
- **Do NOT give the person alcohol or caffeine**
- **Do NOT delay cooling** to take temperature or do other assessments - cool FIRST
- **Do NOT give fluids by mouth** if the person is confused, seizing, or unconscious (choking risk)
- **Do NOT use rubbing alcohol** on the skin for cooling - it can be absorbed through the skin and cause toxicity
- **Do NOT stop cooling efforts** prematurely - continue until clear improvement

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

**Hypothermia - Immediately:**
- Shivering has stopped but the person is not warm (moderate/severe hypothermia)
- Confusion, drowsiness, or loss of consciousness
- Breathing is very slow or irregular
- No improvement after 30 minutes of warming
- Hypothermia in infants, elderly, or people with chronic illness

**Heat Stroke - Immediately (ALWAYS a medical emergency):**
- Body temperature above 40°C / 104°F
- Confusion, delirium, or loss of consciousness
- Seizures
- Hot, dry skin (sweating has stopped)
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Heat exhaustion that does not improve within 30 minutes of cooling

**Risk Factors (Watch These People More Closely):**
- Children under 5 and elderly over 65
- People with chronic diseases (heart disease, diabetes, kidney disease)
- Malnourished individuals
- People taking certain medications (blood pressure medications, diuretics, antidepressants)
- People who are already dehydrated
- Pregnant women

## Prevention in Crisis Settings

**Preventing Hypothermia:**
- Provide adequate blankets and shelter before cold weather
- Keep clothing dry - change wet clothes immediately
- Eat regularly - the body needs calories to generate heat
- Stay together for shared body heat in shelters
- Insulate sleeping areas from the ground
- Watch for hypothermia in injured people, especially those with blood loss

**Preventing Heat Stroke:**
- Drink water frequently - do not wait until thirsty
- Monitor urine color: dark yellow means dehydrated
- Rest in shade during the hottest hours (10am-4pm)
- Wear light, loose-fitting clothing and head coverings
- Never leave children or vulnerable people in closed vehicles or tents without ventilation
- Reduce physical exertion during extreme heat
- Watch for heat exhaustion signs and act early
