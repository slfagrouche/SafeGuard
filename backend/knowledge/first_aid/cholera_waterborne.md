# First Aid: Cholera and Waterborne Diseases

## Overview

Cholera and waterborne diseases (including dysentery and typhoid) are major killers in crisis zones where clean water is disrupted. Cholera can kill within hours if untreated through rapid dehydration. The good news: most cases are treatable with simple oral rehydration if caught early. An estimated 80% of cholera cases can be successfully treated with oral rehydration salts (ORS) alone.

## Recognizing Cholera

**Early Signs (Act Immediately):**
- Sudden onset of profuse, watery diarrhea (often described as "rice-water" appearance - pale, milky)
- Vomiting, often without nausea warning
- Rapid thirst and dry mouth
- Leg cramps and muscle weakness

**Danger Signs (Life-Threatening - Seek Help NOW):**
- Sunken eyes, very dry mouth, no tears when crying (children)
- Skin pinch test: pinch skin on back of hand - if it stays tented for 2+ seconds, severe dehydration
- Lethargy, confusion, or unconsciousness
- No urine output for 6+ hours
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Cold, clammy extremities

**Symptom Timeline:** Symptoms appear 12 hours to 5 days after consuming contaminated water or food.

## Immediate Steps (First 5 Minutes)

1. **Start Rehydration Immediately** - Do not wait for a diagnosis. Begin giving fluids by mouth.
2. **Prepare ORS** (see recipe below) or give any available clean fluid.
3. **Position the Person:** Sitting up or on their side to prevent choking if vomiting.
4. **Isolate Waste:** All diarrhea and vomit is highly infectious. Keep away from water sources.
5. **Wash Your Hands** thoroughly with soap before and after any contact.

## Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) Preparation

### Using WHO ORS Packets (Preferred)
- Dissolve one standard ORS packet in exactly 1 liter of clean water
- Stir until fully dissolved
- Use within 24 hours; discard and make fresh after that

### Homemade ORS (When Packets Unavailable)
**Recipe - measure carefully, too much salt is dangerous:**
- 1 liter (about 4 cups) of clean water (boiled and cooled, or bottled)
- 6 level teaspoons of sugar (about 30 grams)
- 1/2 level teaspoon of salt (about 2.5 grams)

**CRITICAL:** The ratio matters. Too much salt can worsen dehydration. Too much sugar causes more diarrhea. When in doubt, make it slightly more dilute (add more water) - this is safer than too concentrated.

**Taste Test:** The solution should taste like tears - slightly salty but not unpleasant.

### How Much to Give
- **Adults:** Up to 6 liters of ORS on the first day for moderate dehydration. Give small, frequent sips continuously.
- **Children under 5:** 50-100 mL (a few tablespoons) after each loose stool. Continue breastfeeding.
- **If vomiting:** Wait 10 minutes, then restart with small, frequent sips (a teaspoon every 1-2 minutes). Most people can keep down small amounts even while vomiting.

## Ongoing Care Until Help Arrives

1. **Continue ORS** relentlessly - replace every fluid lost. If the person has a bowel movement, give ORS immediately after.
2. **Zinc Supplementation** (if available): For children, a 10-14 day course of zinc tablets reduces diarrhea duration by 25% and stool volume by 30%. Dissolve in water or breast milk for infants.
3. **Monitor Hydration:**
   - Check skin pinch every 30 minutes
   - Monitor urine output (any urine is a good sign)
   - Watch for alertness level changes
4. **Keep Warm:** Dehydration causes body temperature to drop. Cover with blankets.
5. **Continue Feeding:** Once vomiting subsides, give small amounts of bland food. Do not stop feeding children - they need nutrition to recover.
6. **Strict Hygiene:**
   - All caregivers wash hands with soap after any contact
   - Dispose of all diarrhea and vomit safely (bury or latrine, never near water)
   - Disinfect all surfaces the person has touched

## What NOT to Do

- **Do NOT give anti-diarrhea medications** (like loperamide/Imodium) - diarrhea is the body flushing out the bacteria. Stopping it traps the infection.
- **Do NOT give antibiotics without medical guidance** - mass antibiotic use contributes to antimicrobial resistance. Antibiotics are only for severe cases under medical supervision.
- **Do NOT use unclean water** for ORS preparation - this can worsen the illness or spread it.
- **Do NOT wait for a diagnosis** to start rehydration - begin ORS at the first sign of watery diarrhea.
- **Do NOT give sugary drinks** (soda, juice) as a substitute for ORS - the sugar-to-salt ratio is wrong and can worsen diarrhea.
- **Do NOT share cups, utensils, or bedding** with the sick person without thorough cleaning.

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

- Any signs of severe dehydration (sunken eyes, skin pinch stays tented, no urine)
- Person cannot keep any fluids down despite small frequent sips
- Blood in stool (may indicate dysentery rather than cholera)
- Fever above 38.5°C / 101°F (cholera typically does not cause high fever - this may indicate a different infection)
- Children under 5 with any diarrhea lasting more than a few hours
- Pregnant women with diarrhea
- Person becomes confused, very drowsy, or unconscious
- No improvement after 4-6 hours of ORS treatment

## Preventing Waterborne Disease in Crisis Zones

**Water Safety:**
- Boil all drinking water for at least 1 minute (3 minutes at high altitude)
- If boiling is not possible: add 2 drops of unscented household bleach per liter, wait 30 minutes
- Use water purification tablets if available, following package instructions
- Store clean water in covered containers with narrow openings

**Food Safety:**
- Cook food thoroughly - eat while hot
- Wash all fruits and vegetables with clean water
- Avoid raw foods in outbreak areas
- "Boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it"

**Sanitation:**
- Use latrines, never defecate near water sources
- Wash hands with soap after using latrine and before eating or preparing food
- If soap is unavailable, use ash mixed with water as a substitute
- Keep living areas clean and free from human waste

## Other Waterborne Diseases to Watch For

**Typhoid Fever:**
- Prolonged high fever, headache, abdominal pain, constipation OR diarrhea
- Requires antibiotic treatment - seek medical care
- Person may still be contagious after recovery (fecal shedding)

**Dysentery (Bloody Diarrhea):**
- Diarrhea containing blood or mucus
- Often accompanied by fever and abdominal cramps
- Requires medical treatment - more serious than simple diarrhea
- Still give ORS while seeking help

Sources: WHO Cholera Fact Sheet, WHO Diarrhoeal Disease Fact Sheet, WHO/UNICEF ORS guidelines.
