# First Aid: Fractures and Crush Injuries

## Overview

Fractures (broken bones) and crush injuries are extremely common in conflict zones - from building collapses, shelling, vehicle accidents, and falls during evacuation. Most fractures are not immediately life-threatening, but crush injuries carry a hidden danger: **crush syndrome**, which can cause fatal heart failure or kidney failure when a trapped limb is released. Understanding this danger can save lives.

## Recognizing Fractures

**Signs of a Broken Bone:**
- Intense pain at the injury site, especially with movement
- Swelling and bruising (may develop over hours)
- Deformity - limb looks bent, twisted, or shortened compared to the other side
- Inability to use or bear weight on the injured limb
- Grinding or crunching sensation (crepitus) when the area is gently touched
- Numbness or tingling below the injury

**Open (Compound) Fracture:**
- Bone is visible through the skin OR there is a wound near the fracture
- This is more serious due to infection risk and blood loss
- Treat the wound AND the fracture

**Signs That Circulation Is Compromised (Emergency):**
- Pale, blue, or cold fingers/toes below the fracture
- No pulse felt below the fracture (check wrist for arm fractures, top of foot for leg fractures)
- Numbness or inability to move fingers/toes below the fracture
- This means blood supply is being cut off - urgent realignment or medical care needed

## Immediate Steps for Fractures (First 5 Minutes)

### 1. Stop Bleeding First
- If an open fracture is bleeding, apply direct pressure around the wound (not directly over protruding bone)
- Control life-threatening bleeding with tourniquet if necessary (see bleeding guide)
- Bleeding always takes priority over splinting

### 2. Immobilize the Fracture
**The goal is to prevent the broken ends from moving, which reduces pain, prevents further damage, and reduces bleeding.**

- Splint the limb in the position you find it - do not try to straighten or realign
- Immobilize the joint ABOVE and the joint BELOW the fracture
- Example: For a forearm fracture, immobilize the wrist AND elbow

### 3. Check Circulation Below the Fracture
- Before and after splinting, check: Can they feel their fingers/toes? Are fingers/toes warm and pink? Is there a pulse?
- If circulation is absent after splinting, the splint may be too tight - loosen it

## Field Splinting Techniques

### Improvised Splint Materials
Almost anything rigid can serve as a splint:
- Sticks, branches, boards, rolled-up newspapers or magazines
- Cardboard, metal strips, umbrella
- Pillows or folded blankets (soft splint)
- A rolled-up blanket makes an excellent leg splint
- An injured arm can be splinted against the body (sling)
- An injured leg can be splinted to the uninjured leg (buddy splinting)

### How to Apply a Splint
1. **Pad the splint** - place soft material (cloth, clothing) between the splint and skin to prevent pressure sores
2. **Position the splint** along the injured limb, extending past the joints above and below the break
3. **Secure with ties** - use strips of cloth, belts, tape, or bandages at multiple points
4. **Tie firmly but not too tight** - you should be able to slide a finger under the ties
5. **Leave fingers/toes exposed** so you can check circulation (color, warmth, sensation)
6. **Check circulation** every 15-30 minutes after splinting

### Specific Fracture Splinting

**Arm/Wrist Fracture:**
- Splint from elbow to past the fingers
- Place arm in a sling (triangle of cloth tied behind neck)
- Secure sling to body with a second bandage around the chest

**Upper Arm/Shoulder:**
- Immobilize entire arm against body
- Sling plus body wrap
- Do not attempt to move the shoulder joint

**Leg Fracture (Lower Leg):**
- Splint from above the knee to past the ankle
- Use two splints - one on each side of the leg
- Pad around the ankle and knee
- If no splint material: buddy-splint to the other leg with padding between

**Thigh (Femur) Fracture:**
- SERIOUS - femur fractures can cause life-threatening internal bleeding (1-2 liters of blood loss)
- Splint from armpit to past the ankle on the outside, from groin to past ankle on the inside
- Treat for shock (lie flat, elevate legs if possible, keep warm)
- Urgent medical care needed

**Pelvis Fracture:**
- Suspected if pain in hips/groin after fall or crush, unable to bear weight
- Do NOT attempt to splint
- Wrap a sheet or blanket tightly around the hips like a belt to stabilize
- Keep the person still and flat
- Life-threatening internal bleeding is common - treat for shock

**Spinal Fracture:**
- If neck or back pain after trauma, or numbness/weakness in limbs: ASSUME spinal injury
- Do NOT move the person unless in immediate danger
- Keep the head, neck, and body aligned
- If you must move them: log-roll (keep entire body straight, rotate as one unit)
- Support the head at all times

## Tourniquet Use for Fractures

Tourniquets are used for fractures ONLY when:
- An open fracture has life-threatening arterial bleeding that cannot be controlled with direct pressure
- A traumatic amputation has occurred
- See the bleeding control guide for proper tourniquet application
- Write the time of application on the tourniquet or person's skin

## Crush Injuries and Crush Syndrome

### What Is Crush Syndrome?
When a limb is crushed (trapped under rubble, vehicle, collapsed building) for an extended period, muscle tissue dies and releases toxic substances. When the pressure is released, these toxins flood into the bloodstream and can cause:
- **Fatal heart arrhythmia** (from potassium release) - can happen within minutes of release
- **Kidney failure** (from myoglobin blocking kidney tubules)
- **Massive internal fluid shifts** causing shock

This is called "smiling death" because the person may look and feel fine immediately after rescue, then collapse and die suddenly.

### The 15-Minute Rule
**If a person has been trapped/crushed for MORE than 15 minutes, do NOT release the pressure without medical preparation.**

This is counterintuitive - every instinct says "free them immediately" - but releasing without preparation can kill them faster than leaving them trapped.

### Recognizing Crush Injury Risk
- Person trapped under heavy object (rubble, vehicle, beam) for more than 15 minutes
- Limb is compressed, pale, cool, or numb
- After release: dark/red-brown urine (myoglobin - a critical danger sign)
- The "5 P's" of compartment syndrome: **P**ain, **P**allor (pale), **P**aresthesia (tingling), **P**aralysis, **P**ulselessness

### If Person Has Been Trapped LESS Than 15 Minutes
- Remove the crushing object as quickly as possible
- Treat any bleeding and fractures
- Splint injured limbs
- Monitor and transport to medical care

### If Person Has Been Trapped MORE Than 15 Minutes

**Before Releasing the Crush:**
1. **Call for medical help** - ideally, an IV line should be started BEFORE releasing the crush
2. **If IV fluids are available:** Begin rapid normal saline infusion (500 mL/hour or faster) BEFORE releasing the crush. This dilutes the toxins.
3. **Apply a tourniquet** to the crushed limb ABOVE the crush site BEFORE releasing the weight. This temporarily prevents toxins from reaching the heart and kidneys.
4. **Keep the person warm** and treat for anticipated shock

**During Release:**
1. Remove the crushing weight
2. If tourniquet was applied, it buys time but the limb still needs blood flow eventually - get to medical care rapidly
3. Begin aggressive fluid replacement if available

**After Release:**
1. **Monitor closely** - the danger period is the first 1-6 hours after release
2. Watch for: irregular heartbeat, chest pain, confusion, dark urine, no urine output
3. **Continue fluids** - target high urine output to flush myoglobin from kidneys
4. Keep the person warm and lying flat
5. **Do NOT elevate the crushed limb** - keep it at heart level
6. Transport to medical care as urgently as possible

## What NOT to Do

**Fractures:**
- **Do NOT try to push bone back** under the skin in an open fracture
- **Do NOT try to straighten or realign** a deformed fracture - splint as you find it
- **Do NOT apply a splint too tightly** - check circulation regularly
- **Do NOT remove shoes or boots** from a suspected ankle/foot fracture - the shoe acts as a splint and swelling may prevent getting it back on
- **Do NOT give food or water** if surgery may be needed
- **Do NOT attempt to move someone** with suspected spinal injury unless they are in immediate danger

**Crush Injuries:**
- **Do NOT release a crushed limb** (trapped more than 15 minutes) without medical preparation if at all possible
- **Do NOT apply heat** to crushed or recently freed limbs
- **Do NOT massage** crushed limbs - this pushes toxins into the bloodstream faster
- **Do NOT elevate** a recently freed crushed limb above heart level
- **Do NOT ignore dark/red-brown urine** after crush release - this is a medical emergency (myoglobinuria)

## When to Seek Professional Help Urgently

**Immediate:**
- Any open fracture (bone visible or wound near fracture)
- Loss of circulation below a fracture (cold, pale, pulseless limb)
- Femur (thigh) or pelvis fracture (high risk of life-threatening bleeding)
- Any suspected spinal injury
- Any crush injury where the person was trapped more than 15 minutes
- Dark or red-brown urine after crush release
- Signs of shock (rapid pulse, pale skin, confusion)
- Multiple fractures

**Urgent:**
- Any fracture that has been splinted (needs proper setting/casting)
- Increasing pain, swelling, or numbness despite splinting
- Fractures in children (may involve growth plates)

**Watch For (Hours to Days After):**
- Compartment syndrome: increasing pain DESPITE splinting, pain with passive stretching of fingers/toes, swelling that makes the limb feel tight and hard
- Infection in open fractures: redness, warmth, pus, fever, red streaks
- Fat embolism (rare, after long bone fractures): confusion, shortness of breath, rash on chest 24-72 hours after fracture
