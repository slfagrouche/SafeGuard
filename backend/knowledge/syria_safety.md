# Syria Safety Information

## Current Conflict Context

Syria's conflict began in 2011 and has involved multiple armed groups, international forces, and widespread displacement. The situation varies dramatically by region, with different areas under control of different forces.

**Control Areas (approximate, highly fluid):**
- **Syrian Government**: Damascus, central/western Syria, Aleppo city, parts of south
- **Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF/Kurdish-led)**: Northeast Syria (Hasakah, parts of Raqqa, Deir ez-Zor)
- **Turkish-backed forces**: Northern Aleppo, Afrin, parts of northeast
- **Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS)**: Parts of Idlib governorate
- Situation has evolved significantly - verify current control areas

**Most Affected Areas:**
- **Idlib**: Last major opposition-held area, periodic bombardment, massive IDP population
- **Northeast Syria**: Cross-border dynamics, intermittent military operations
- **Deir ez-Zor**: ISIS remnant attacks, tribal tensions
- **Aleppo**: Reconstruction ongoing but significant infrastructure damage
- **Daraa**: Periodic security operations and reconciliation breakdowns

**Relatively Safer Areas:**
- Parts of coastal Syria (Latakia, Tartous)
- Some areas of Damascus suburbs (post-reconciliation)
- Parts of central Syria (Homs city center after reconstruction)

- Damascus: +963-11-339-4971

- Branches in all accessible governorates
- Community kitchens and health services

**Medecins Sans Frontieres (MSF):**
- Active in northwest Syria (Idlib) and northeast Syria
- Emergency medical care, hospital support
- Maternal and child health programs

**UNHCR Syria:**
- Damascus Office: +963-11-213-8600
- Protection services, shelter support, winterization
- IDP registration and assistance

**World Food Programme (WFP):**
- Food assistance to millions across Syria
- Cross-border and cross-line deliveries
- Bread distribution networks in urban areas

**White Helmets (Syria Civil Defence):**
- Search and rescue in opposition-held areas
- Emergency response to airstrikes and bombardment
- First responders in northwest Syria

## Health and Medical Access

**Functioning Hospitals:**
- **Damascus**: Al-Mouwasat Hospital, Damascus Hospital, Tishreen Military Hospital, Children's Hospital
- **Aleppo**: Aleppo University Hospital (Al-Razi), Al-Kindi Hospital
- **Homs**: Al-Bassel Hospital, National Hospital Homs
- **Latakia**: Tishreen University Hospital, Al-Assad Hospital
- **Idlib**: Bab al-Hawa Hospital, multiple MSF-supported facilities
- **Northeast (Hasakah/Qamishli)**: Al-Hasakah National Hospital, MSF-supported clinics

**Critical Health Concerns:**
- Earthquake aftermath (2023) still affecting northwest - infrastructure damaged
- Cholera outbreaks recurring since 2022
- Leishmaniasis endemic in conflict-affected areas
- Severe shortage of medications, especially in besieged/hard-to-reach areas
- Mental health crisis across population

**Healthcare Access:**
- Different health systems operate in different control areas
- Cross-line medical referrals extremely difficult
- Many health facilities destroyed or non-functional
- NGO-supported facilities critical in opposition areas

## Water and Food Security

**Water:**
- Major water infrastructure damaged across the country
- Alouk water station (northeast) subject to disruptions affecting 460,000+ people
- Damascus water from Barada spring - periodic contamination
- UNICEF supports water trucking and rehabilitation in critical areas

**Food:**
- WFP food baskets and bread distribution
- Local markets functional but prices extremely high
- Syrian Pound severely devalued making basics unaffordable
- Fuel shortages affect food transport and cooking

## Displacement and Shelter

**IDP Camps (Northwest Syria):**
- Over 1,300 IDP camps and informal settlements in Idlib/northwest
- Many camps overcrowded with minimal services
- Winter conditions severe - tents inadequate
- Flooding common in poorly sited camps

**IDP Concentrations:**
- **Idlib governorate**: Over 1.5 million IDPs
- **Aleppo**: Large IDP populations in city and surrounding areas
- **Northeast**: Multiple IDP camps (Al-Hol, Roj, others)
- **Damascus suburbs**: Returnees and IDPs mixed

**Refugee Returns:**
- Some returns occurring from neighboring countries
- Conditions for safe return often not met
- Property and documentation issues for returnees
- Security screening at government checkpoints

## Security and Movement

**Checkpoints:**
- Extensive checkpoint networks by all parties
- Government, SDF, Turkish-backed forces each operate checkpoints
- Carry all available identification
- Military service documents critical for men of military age
- Do not photograph checkpoints or military sites

**Landmines and UXO:**
- Massive contamination across the country
- Particularly dangerous: eastern Aleppo, Raqqa, Deir ez-Zor, Daraa
- Stay on paved roads
- Never touch unknown objects
- UNMAS and other organizations conducting clearance

**Communication:**
- Internet available but monitored
- VPN use may be risky depending on area
- Multiple mobile networks (Syriatel, MTN Syria)
- Power outages frequent - 2-4 hours electricity per day in many areas

## Specific Risks

**Conscription:**
- Military-age men (18-42) subject to mandatory military service
- Checkpoints specifically check military service status
- Significant risk for men traveling between areas

**Arbitrary Detention:**
- Risk at checkpoints and security offices
- Document all movements when possible
- Inform family of travel plans
- Know location of ICRC offices for tracing services

**Gender-Based Violence:**
- Widespread in displacement settings and all areas
- Services available through UNFPA, local NGOs
- Safe spaces for women in some camp and urban settings

## Financial Considerations

**Currency:**
- Syrian Pound severely devalued (from ~50 to 15,000+ per USD)
- US dollars widely preferred for larger transactions
- Turkish Lira used in Turkish-controlled areas
- Hawala money transfer system most reliable
- ATMs rarely functional - cash economy dominant

Remember: Syria's situation varies enormously by region. Each area has different controlling forces, different humanitarian access, and different security dynamics. Always verify current conditions for your specific location with local contacts and humanitarian organizations.
